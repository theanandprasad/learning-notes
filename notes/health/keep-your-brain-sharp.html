How to Keep Your Brain Sharp: A Practical Playbook Beyond the Basics

By Dr. Tommy Wood (guest post on Tim Ferriss's blog) • Published Apr 24, 2026 • Saved Jun 9, 2026
Brain Health Dementia Prevention Longevity Sleep Nutrients

"The era of preventive neurology has arrived." At least 45% of dementia may be preventable — so beyond the big lifestyle rocks, here are the lesser-known, low-effort, high-leverage levers, with the actual lab targets and studies to anchor them.

⚠️ Health information, not medical advice. A guest post by neuroscientist Dr. Tommy Wood (promoting his book The Stimulated Mind); it names many specific supplements, doses, tests, and consumer products. Lab targets and figures are as cited in the article — talk to your own doctor before testing, supplementing, or changing anything. Captured to preserve the specifics, not as a prescription.

The frame: dementia is increasingly preventable

The Lancet Commission estimates ~45% of dementia is preventable via 14 modifiable risk factors: low education, hearing loss, vision loss, high LDL cholesterol, brain trauma, physical inactivity, diabetes, smoking, high blood pressure, obesity, excessive alcohol, social isolation, depression, and air pollution. Other work puts it above 70%. This playbook adds the emerging, lower-effort levers on top of the lifestyle basics.

1. B vitamins + Omega-3 (they work as a pair)

Missing from the Lancet list. Homocysteine-lowering B vitamins (B12, folate/B9, plus B6 and riboflavin/B2) and Omega-3s are each only effective when the other is adequate — that's why studying them individually fails. The VITACOG trial (B12 + folic acid + B6 in people with elevated homocysteine) slowed brain atrophy and cognitive decline — but only in those with adequate Omega-3 status; multiple trials since show the same interdependence.

Strategy + doses

If homocysteine > 11, consider the VITACOG doses: 800 mcg folic acid (1,360 DFE) + 500 mcg B12, plus ~20 mg B6 and 1–2 mg riboflavin. For Omega-3, average 2–4 g/day EPA + DHA (seafood or supplement). Named B-vitamin options: Thorne Methyl-Guard, Pure Encapsulations MethylAssist, Designs for Health Homocysteine Supreme. Omega-3: Nordic Naturals Ultimate Omega 2X, Parasol Omega-3, Momentous.

2. Toxins in your air, water, and food

Air. Pollution (roads, industry, wildfires) raises dementia risk via inflammation/oxidative stress. Wear an N95 on smoky days (filters the ~2.5-micron particles). Indoor air is often several times worse than outdoor → a HEPA air purifier where you spend time (one study found a HEPA filter lowered blood pressure). Bonus: the air-pollution effect was attenuated in people with good B-vitamin intake.
Water. Mostly safe, but watch lead (millions of lead pipes remain) and PFAS ("forever chemicals" — in food packaging, old/damaged nonstick cookware, drinking water; they accumulate in the brain). Even a basic water filter removes most heavy metals and PFAS.
Microplastics. The viral "a credit card's worth per week" claim was reanalyzed down to <0.0001% of that, and brain measurements (via pyrolysis) are debated (heated fat can look like polyethylene). Microplastics in dementia brains may be a consequence, not a cause. Still: bisphenols/phthalates are linked to harm, so reduce exposure — filtered tap (not bottled) water, wood/metal utensils, glass for storing/microwaving, fresh foods. And eat more fiber and berries (anthocyanins + β-glucan may blunt plastic/PFAS effects; berries independently improve cognition).

Tim's add-on: the hotel CO₂ problem

James Nestor (author of Breath) travels with an Aranet4 CO₂ monitor and finds many hotels — especially "green"/LEED ones — seal windows and recirculate stale air, recording CO₂ up to 2,800 ppm (outdoor ≈ 425 ppm; cognitive scores drop significantly past ~1,500 ppm). Fix: call ahead and make "windows that open, even a few inches" a booking criterion.

Set-and-forget gear named: air — HealthMate, Jaspr, Blueair, Coway Airmega (budget). Water — check EPA/EWG, then a jug (Epic, ClearlyFiltered, Brita Elite, or Cyclopure for PFAS) or under-sink reverse osmosis.

3. Oral health is brain health

Periodontal disease (gingivitis → periodontitis) comes from an oral-microbiome imbalance (overgrowth of P. gingivalis, S. mutans), opening a route for mouth bacteria to enter the body.

Strategy: brush ≥2 min, 2×/day; clear between teeth (floss / water flosser / interdental brushes); dentist/hygienist ≥2×/year and treat periodontitis promptly. For periodontitis, consider xylitol gum (2–3 g, 2–3×/day) or mouthwash (TheraBreath, ACT; gums: Epic, Kaigum).

4. Cognitive training that actually works

The landmark ACTIVE study (2,800 adults 65+) randomized people to control or one of three trainings — processing speed, reasoning, or memory — for ten ~60-min sessions over 5–6 weeks plus boosters. Five years on, all three trained groups declined less in quality of life, with the biggest benefit from processing-speed training. A 20-year follow-up found processing-speed training (with boosters) significantly lowered dementia risk.

5. Protect what you have: hearing, vision, illness

If stimulus drives cognition, losing inputs hurts. Hearing and vision loss raise dementia risk (less input + less engagement) — and it's reversible (hearing aids, cataract surgery). More insidious is accumulated illness: hospitalization accelerates cognitive decline (worse illness, worse decline); even two weeks of bed rest impairs cognition in healthy people. Each bout costs "a little fitness, a little muscle, a little cognitive function" — which adds up and masquerades as "just aging."

The shingles-vaccine natural experiment

Wales rolled out the shingles vaccine by date of birth: born before Sept 1, 1933 → ~0% vaccinated; just after → ~50% vaccinated. The eligible group saw a sudden drop in shingles diagnoses and a sudden drop in dementia diagnoses — replicated in Australia, the US, and Canada. Strategy: get hearing/visual aids when needed; take vaccines and early treatment for flu and shingles to minimize time "out of action."

6. Sleep: the clearest missing factor

Poor sleep was the starkest omission from the Lancet report, and it's a well-established dementia risk. Beyond the basics (cool dark room, morning light + dim evenings, warm bath before bed, sleep mask, magnesium, enough time in bed, and good ventilation — CO₂ climbs in closed rooms and degrades deep sleep), some lesser-known levers:

Hemoglobin testing (while you're at the lab)

Get a CBC + iron panel. Anemia (Hb < 12 g/dL women / < 13 men) is linked to higher dementia risk (often low iron, sometimes B vitamins or inflammation; common cause of perimenopausal fatigue/brain-fog). High Hb (> 15 women / > 17 men) often signals obstructive sleep apnea — itself a dementia risk, treatable with CPAP.

If the majority of dementia cases truly are preventable, a sensible approach is to tackle our own individual risk factors and stack the deck in our favor.— Dr. Tommy Wood
