How to Do What You Love

By Paul Graham • Published January 2006 • Saved Jun 5, 2026
Work & Meaning Career Prestige Life Design

"Do what you love" is four words of advice that's almost useless on its own — because doing what you love is complicated. This essay is about the complications.

The throughline

To do something well you have to like it — so the most successful people genuinely like their work. But most of us are misled in childhood (work = pain), then lured off course by prestige and money. Finding work you love is rare and hard; the discipline isn't in forcing the work, it's in the search. Keep "what you want" separate from "what seems possible," and always produce.

The trap: work was defined as not-fun

As kids, work and fun seemed like opposites by definition. School was a "dilute version of work" meant to prepare us for the real thing, and teachers — for whom work mostly wasn't fun — implicitly taught that work isn't fun. By the time you're old enough to choose, you've absorbed the lesson and think: "I am not like these people; I am not suited to this world."

Even after work broke free from "making a living" for Graham in college, his idea of work still carried "a large component of pain" — the belief that hard problems can't be fun, so you must force yourself. "If you think something's supposed to hurt, you're less likely to notice if you're doing it wrong."

Bounds: how much should you like your work?

If you don't know how much you're supposed to like your work, you'll stop searching too early — and end up with something chosen by your parents, by money, by prestige, or by inertia. Graham sets two bounds:

Sirens: prestige and money

Two forces warp your sense of what you enjoy. Prestige is the opinion of people whose judgment you have no reason to respect — yet it's "a powerful magnet that warps even your beliefs about what you enjoy," causing you to work not on what you like but on what you'd like to like (the would-be novelist who loves reading novels but not the actual work of "making up elaborate lies").

Prestige is just fossilized inspiration. If you do anything well enough, you'll make it prestigious… So just do what you like, and let prestige take care of itself.

Two practical heuristics on prestige:

  • "It might be a good rule simply to avoid any prestigious task. If it didn't suck, they wouldn't have had to make it prestigious."
  • If you admire two kinds of work equally but one is more prestigious, choose the other — your admiration for the less prestigious one is probably more genuine.

Money alone isn't that dangerous (no one's tempted by well-paid but disdained work like telemarketing). The danger is money + prestige together — corporate law, medicine — "dangerously tempting to someone young, who hasn't thought much about what they really like."

Parents tend to err toward money — not because they're materialistic, but because, as parents, they "share risks more than rewards."

Discipline: the real work is the search

So few find work they love (Graham guesses a few hundred thousand out of billions) that you shouldn't underestimate the task — or feel bad if you haven't succeeded. Admitting you're discontented already puts you ahead of those still in denial. The paradox:

Doing great work takes less discipline than people think — because the way to do great work is to find something you like so much that you don't have to force yourself. But finding work you love does usually require discipline.

Careers more often have "the trajectory of a ping-pong ball" (study A, drop out to do B, become famous for C) than glide along railroad tracks. Two tests to stay honest:

Do a good job anyway

Try to do well at whatever you're doing, even if you don't like it — so you know you're not using dissatisfaction as an excuse for laziness, and you build the habit of doing things well.

Always produce

Are you actually writing pages, however bad? Producing keeps you from using "the grand novel I'll write one day" as an opiate.

"Always produce" will discover your life's work the way water, with the aid of gravity, finds the hole in your roof.

And keep two questions separate: what you want to work on vs. what seems possible. It's painful to see the gap, so most people pre-emptively lower their expectations — "Oh, I can't draw" is a statement of intention ("I'm not going to try"), not fact.

Since it's hard to get paid for work you love, there are two routes:

Don't decide too soon

Kids who know early what they want seem impressive — "but odds are it's wrong." Graham's cautionary example: the determined high-schooler who became a doctor, overcame every obstacle "including, unfortunately, not liking it," and now has "a life chosen for her by a high-school kid." You must decide on "ridiculously incomplete information," so use flexible media: pick work that could become either an organic or two-job career, and early on seek jobs that let you do many different things.

Beware the fantasy of "make money first, find meaning later." Constraints give life shape — look at lottery winners. "The happiest people are not those who have [financial security], but those who like what they do."

The home stretch

Whichever route you take, expect a struggle… But if you have the destination in sight you'll be more likely to arrive at it. If you know you can love work, you're in the home stretch, and if you know what work you love, you're practically there.
