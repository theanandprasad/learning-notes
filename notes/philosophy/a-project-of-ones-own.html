A Project of One's Own

By Paul Graham • Published June 2021 • Saved Jun 9, 2026
Work Autonomy Creativity Meaning Motivation

A nine-year-old who can't wait to get home to write his story has discovered something most adults spend their lives trying to get back: a way of working that feels less like work than like skating.

The core

Working on a project of your own is, in PG's words, "as different from ordinary work as skating is from walking" — more fun, but also much more productive. A lot of great work has been done by people skating in this sense. The trick is making this childhood mode of working converge with adult work, which two things make hard: the conditions you choose to work under, and your own morale.

Skating, not walking

PG noticed his nine-year-old son couldn't wait to get home to work on his story. And what he felt wasn't worry that the boy was wasting his time — it was happiness that he'd discovered this way of working. Working on a project of your own is to ordinary work what skating is to walking: it gets you there faster and you enjoy the ride.

The feeling isn't exactly happiness — better words are excited and engaged. When PG is writing an essay he's mostly worried and puzzled: groping for an idea, unsure he can pin it down. The happiness arrives in brief moments when something works — and then it's straight on to the next problem. So why do it? Because to people who like working this way, nothing else feels as right. It's like being an animal in its natural habitat — awake and alive.

The concealed path from the treehouse

Kids experience this mode naturally. The hard part is making it converge with adult work — and our customs make that harder than it needs to be. We treat "playing" and "hobbies" as qualitatively different from "work." A kid building a treehouse isn't told he's on a (long) route to architecture or engineering. Instead we conceal that route and tell him the path to such work runs through school.

But schoolwork, PG observes, "is usually neither a project, nor one's own." The work that made Darwin and Newton famous was far closer in spirit to building treehouses than to studying for exams. If forced to choose, PG would pick a kid working on ambitious projects of their own over a kid with good grades — and not on sentiment. From years of picking startups for Y Combinator, he's found that projects have more predictive value than grades.

Two senses of "one's own"

Work can be one's own in two distinct senses, and they behave very differently.

(1) Doing it voluntarily — being pulled rather than pushed. This is a sharp edge. But the test isn't simply whether you were told to do it: you can choose to do something you were told to do, and own it more thoroughly than whoever told you. PG's mathematician father treated the problems in a math book as the point, and the surrounding text as mere annotation — the assignment became fully his.

(2) Doing it by yourself — this is a soft edge that shades into collaboration in two ways: (a) a shared project, where two people work on one thing (two mathematicians on a single proof); and (b) a jigsaw, where separate projects fit together (one person writes the text, another does the graphic design).

Keeping it alive inside an organization

Under the right conditions, the excitement of a project of one's own survives for a while even inside an organization. The history of successful organizations is partly the history of techniques for preserving it. PG's example is the original Macintosh team — Burrell Smith, Andy Hertzfeld, Bill Atkinson, Susan Kare. They worked late into the night not because they were driven, but because they individually felt the excitement of a project of their own.

They weren't tennis balls hit by Steve Jobs, but rockets let loose by Steve Jobs.

That's the opposite end of the spectrum from sweatshop-style long hours. Which is why PG thinks it's a mistake to insist dogmatically on "work/life balance." For skaters, the relation between work and life is better described by a dash than a slash. PG wouldn't work on anything he didn't want to take over his life.

Newness, autonomy, and the wormhole

Newness helps work feel like your own. It's why programmers so often rewrite things from scratch — costly in characters, but writing it yourself is so much more rewarding that a good programmer can end up net ahead. Capitalism, conveniently, encourages exactly this rewriting. Skating also aligns naturally with solving new problems — part of why startup payoffs are high: there's a higher market price for unsolved problems, plus a productivity discount, and clean-sheet work is easier to recruit skaters for.

But all of this requires autonomy. You can't be working to order, or be slowed down by bureaucracy. There are two ways to have no boss: be the boss (a startup), or work on projects outside work (open source). Financially these sit at opposite ends — yet both are often run by skaters, and there's a wormhole between them: working on a project just for fun is one of the best ways to find startup ideas.

The hardest part is morale — and the beginning

For projects that don't make money, the hardest part is morale. Adults have it harder than kids, who simply plunge in without worrying whether they're wasting time, or how their treehouse compares to anyone else's.

And the single most important phase is the beginning — the move from "it might be cool to do x" to actually doing x. Here, high standards are "not merely useless but positively harmful." Far more people are deterred by fear of failure than ever start too many projects. The remedy is to remember, as grownups, that our projects trace right back to treehouses — and to recapture the careless confidence we had as kids.

The ideal posture

Be deliberate in choosing which projects to take on — but carelessly confident in starting them. Care about the what; refuse to let fear of failure govern the whether.
