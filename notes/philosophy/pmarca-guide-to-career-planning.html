The Pmarca Guide to Career Planning

By Marc Andreessen — blog.pmarca.com (via Pmarchive) • Published Sept–Oct 2007 • Saved Jun 5, 2026
Career Opportunity & Risk Skills Silicon Valley

A four-part guide for high-potential people who want to make a significant impact — biased toward fast-moving, high-tech fields. Consolidated here from the original series.

0 · Introduction 1 · Opportunity 2 · Skills & Education 3 · Where to Go & Why

The guide in one breath

Don't plan your career — build skills and chase opportunities. Treat your working life as a portfolio of risks taken across decades, not standalone bets. Make yourself rare by stacking a double/triple threat of skills. Then go where the action is: a young industry whose founders are still alive, the center of that industry, inside a high-growth company.

Part 0Introduction: who this is for

Andreessen admits he dislikes giving career advice in real life — "most people who say they want career planning advice aren't actually looking for advice; they just want validation of the path they've already chosen." But he has opinions, so the series lays them out. The framing/disclaimers:

  • Aimed at high-potential people who want to excel and make a significant impact — not for those whose top priority is work/life balance or lifestyle (plenty of other resources exist for that).
  • Biased toward high-tech / Silicon Valley and similar fields — fast-moving, rapidly changing, lots of new companies and opportunity.
  • Words like "ambition," "promotion," "advancement" apply equally to purely technical careers — read them as gaining latitude to lead bigger/more complex work, not just managing people.
  • It's all personal opinion — "the things I would want to know if I were entering college today."

Part 1Opportunity

The first rule of career planning: Do not plan your career.— Marc Andreessen

The world is too complex and changes too fast to predict your industries, companies, roles, or contributions. Trying to plan only frustrates you and blinds you to the significant opportunities life throws your way. "Career planning = career limiting." The second rule: instead, focus on developing skills and pursuing opportunities.

Your career is a portfolio

Like an investor, don't judge each job in isolation — judge it as part of an overall portfolio of jobs/roles/opportunities across a 50+ year span. Each has a potential return (income, skills, experience, location…) and a risk profile (getting fired, a move, bankruptcy, opportunity cost). What matters is how the risks and returns combine, which means individual high-risk bets can be smart if the return is good enough. Most people don't think this way — they evaluate each opportunity standalone ("boy, that looks risky") instead of asking whether it fits strategically into the whole portfolio.

On risk (the financial, analyzable kind — not getting hit by an Escalade): "without taking risk, you can't exploit any opportunities." You can live a quiet, happy life — but you're unlikely to create something new or make your mark. The trader's test (Aaron Brown): hire the person who wanted something obtainable only by taking a risk — who knew the risk, respected it, and took it anyway.

What risks to take, and when

Closing nudge (Taleb, paraphrased): opportunities are rarer than you think — when a real one opens, cancel your other plans; the window may not open again.

Part 2Skills & Education

What to study

Contrary to "college is your one chance to pursue your passion," Andreessen argues: if you intend to have an impact, start building concrete, real-world skills as fast as possible — pursue your passion after you're primed for success. A liberal-arts degree alone is "almost useless" for entering the workforce with impact; pair it with a useful graduate degree, or better, get a substantive undergrad degree.

Useful undergrad degrees have a technical element: engineering, computer science, physics, chemistry, math, economics. They teach you to do something hard and useful, to think with reason, logic, and data, and they signal seriousness. "Like heading into the real world armed with an assault rifle instead of a dull knife."

Graduate degrees: overrated if you have a useful undergrad degree (you can hit the workforce and progress on ability). If your undergrad isn't useful, a practical grad degree is a great idea. Best combo: an MBA on top of engineering/math. Most high-impact people (outside pure research) don't have PhDs.

Where to study: try hard to attend one of the best schools in the world for your field — "always be in the best pond possible." Can't start there? Get great grades and transfer, or do a graduate degree at a top school. Even starting at community college, 4–6 years can vault you into the top tier. While in school: work during the year (research jobs, campus labs) and stack internships/co-ops at the best companies — graduate with real experience and a demonstrated work ethic.

The double/triple threat

Capitalism rewards things that are both rare and valuable. You make yourself rare by combining two or more "pretty goods" until no one else has your mix.— Scott Adams (quoted by Andreessen), paraphrased framing

Five post-school skills worth stacking onto your degree(s):

1 · Communication — written or verbal; most people are terrible at it because they never treat it as a skill (especially engineers). Communication + another useful skill makes you "the boss of the people who have only one skill."
2 · Management — learn from a great manager, then get responsibility for a team. Even if you won't manage much, you'll understand why companies and people work the way they do.
3 · Sales — not steak knives: the art of getting people to do what's genuinely in their interest, predictably. Helps with recruiting, fundraising, partnerships, press — even marriage. (John Doerr called his year "carrying a bag" at Intel the most valuable of his life.)
4 · Finance — financial statements, budgeting, how debt & equity markets work. Helps your career, personal life, and any company you start. Read the FT and WSJ cover to cover.
5 · International — time on the ground in other cultures pays off; lean toward younger, fast-growing economies. Almost every interesting business now has a strong international element.

The "Organization Kid" warning

Drawing on David Brooks's essay, Andreessen warns elite, over-orchestrated graduates: you may reach 22+ having worked hard your whole life but never truly challenged yourself — always inside a parental/educational safety net, never making tough decisions on incomplete information and living with the consequences. Get into the real world and risk visible failure — not a B or C, but a boss yelling at you, a product that tanks, a missed ship date, getting fired. Learning to get smacked by reality and get right back up "may be the most valuable skill you can ever learn."

Part 3Where to Go & Why

Get to the center — fast

Once you've picked an industry, head straight for its core of change and opportunity; don't detour for lucrative-but-peripheral roles. "Never worry about being a small fish in a big pond" — being a big fish in a small pond means hitting the ceiling fast while nobody cares. Apply this to company (go where the action is), to startup (aim at the largest, fastest-growing opportunity), and to city:

Start at a young, high-growth company

In a fast-changing field, the best first job is a younger, high-growth company — not a big company (which teaches you only how big companies work) and usually not a raw startup yet (too variable when you're starting out). What you get:

  • You'll do lots of stuff → skills and experience fast.
  • You'll get promoted quickly → growth creates a chronic shortage of leaders.
  • You'll adapt to high energy and high expectations → "training for a marathon while wearing ankle weights."
  • Reputational benefit → the right company on your résumé is "as valuable as any advanced degree."

Caveat: don't be a "summertime soldier" who joins something successful then bails when it gets hard — hiring managers read that off your résumé dates forever. High-growth companies always hit potholes; gutting through them is key to your education. Then go to a startup (pick one where you get the product, see a large market, and respect the people) — or start your own. If it fails, try again; reset at a high-growth company if needed. "Repeat as necessary until you change the world."

Every job is tactical and strategic

Tactical

Kick ass: gain skills and experiences, and do it so well people sing your praises for decades.

Strategic (often overlooked)

Learn how the business and the industry actually work. Ask: how would I start a firm like this today? What assumptions underlie everyone's behavior — and should they change? Who's underserved? What tech could change everything? "If the creators of this industry were starting out today, what would they be doing now?"

Andreessen ends with a line from Damages: a mentor advises the young attorney to learn everything she can from her ruthless boss — "then get the hell out of there before it's too late."
