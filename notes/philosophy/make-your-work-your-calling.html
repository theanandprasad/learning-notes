Make Your Work Your Calling

By Arthur C. Brooks — The Atlantic ("How to Build a Life") • Published June 26, 2025 • Saved Jun 5, 2026
Work & Meaning Calling Purpose Intrinsic Motivation

Don't wait for your life's calling to find you with the perfect job. Turn whatever job you find into the way you seek that calling.

The core move

A calling isn't a perfect job you discover — it's a way of working you create. The secret is to make your work, whatever it is, your calling, through three steps: look within (intrinsic reward), focus on fascination (interest), and be that person (serve others).

A companion to the work-and-meaning cluster: Paul Graham's "How to Do What You Love" and Tim Urban's "How to Pick a Career."

Chop wood, carry water

Brooks opens with a Zen story. A novice monk is told that before enlightenment he will "chop wood and carry water." For decades he does exactly that — backbreaking work — dreaming that once he becomes a master his calling will be something finer: teaching, contemplation, prayer. Finally he attains the rank of master and asks what his job will be now.

Across traditions, work is framed as inherently meaningful: in Genesis, God places Adam in Eden "to dress it and to keep it" — designed to work, even in paradise; Hinduism teaches that "by performing one's natural occupation, one worships the Creator."

The three steps to make any work a calling

1Look within

Home in on intrinsic compensation — the psychological reward of the work itself — rather than only extrinsic compensation (wage, benefits, prestige). You need the extrinsic to pay rent, but the intrinsic is what gives meaning.

Intrinsic

The inherent reward of doing the work. People who are intrinsically motivated like their job more, work harder, and stick with it longer.

Extrinsic

Wage, benefits, prestige — necessary, but not a source of meaning.

It generalizes beyond work: students do puzzles longer for fun than for course credit; a relationship is better when you do kind things out of love rather than to win favor.

2Focus on fascination

The intrinsic reward most tied to calling is interest — a basic positive emotion with an evolutionary root (curious ancestors explored, prospered, and passed on their genes). Seek work that is intrinsically interesting to you; interest is deeply personal (one of his sons loves data science, the other construction management — neither can imagine the other's work).

Even a job taken out of desperation has interesting facets. A musician friend working food service while auditioning made his work interesting by observing the people around him "as if he were an anthropologist," journaling his observations at night.

3Be that person

A second intrinsic reward is service to others. Workplaces with a strong culture of helping and reciprocity show the highest satisfaction and morale — and the impulse to help your co-workers raises your own job satisfaction. Help others, and the job becomes more like a calling.

To the young man who felt like a drudge in a cubicle farm, Brooks advised small, unbidden acts of kindness: bring the guy in the next cubicle a fresh coffee after lunch and notice his reaction; write an appreciation email for no reason. "Being that person" changes how you see your own role.

The reframe

Brooks feared that switching from music to social science would just reveal him as a chronic malcontent — but his current work genuinely feels like his calling. The deeper realization: had he applied these three steps as a young musician, he could have found meaning there too — appreciating the great music, taking interest in its history, lightening his fellow musicians' load.

To find a calling is not about the actual work of chopping wood and carrying water. The sense of calling comes in how we make the act of chopping wood meaningful, and in how we serve others by the water we're carrying. That is the path to true enlightenment.
