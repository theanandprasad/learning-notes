How to Pick a Career (That Actually Fits You)

By Tim Urban — Wait But Why • Published April 2018 • Saved Jun 5, 2026
Career Decision-Making First Principles Self-Knowledge

Your career will eat 20–60% of your waking adult life — yet most of us choose it on autopilot, guided by advice that's "your great uncle at the holidays… 45 years out of date." This is a framework for choosing like a chef, not a cook.

The whole framework in one breath

Reason like a chef (from first principles), not a cook (copying recipes). Audit your true desires (the Yearning Octopus), strip out imposter wants, rank what's left on a priority shelf, and find where your honest Want Box overlaps your honest Reality Box. Then stop trying to plan 40 years — plan only the next dot, treat it as an experiment, and iterate.

A companion to Paul Graham's "How to Do What You Love" and Derek Sivers' "Hell Yeah or No" — the three together cover why work matters, how to filter commitments, and how to actually choose.

The starting problem: cook vs. chef

Society hands you a recipe for careers — a snapshot of an old, now-outdated game. A cook follows that recipe (reasoning by analogy: copy, conform, tweak). A chef reasons from first principles — raw facts puzzled together independently, like a scientist. Career decisions demand chef-level thinking, because the world changes faster than the conventional wisdom describing it.

Mapping the goal: Want Box ∩ Reality Box

Your viable careers live where two circles overlap. The catch: both are drawn from your perceived reality, which is usually wrong on both sides.

Unmasking imposters — the "Why Game" & Denial Prison

Many wants are imposters wearing a mask — installed by your mom, conventional wisdom, broader culture, famous philosophers, or "7-year-old you." Play the Why Game: ask "Why do I want this?" repeatedly and trace each answer to its origin.

Three outcomes

Authentic — a long chain of your own independent thought. Imposter — traces back to someone else's installation. Ambiguous — lost in haze.

Denial Prison

The deepest level, where genuine yearnings get locked away — too painful to face, or imprisoned by a parent's values. (A real love of carpentry jailed by an installed belief that prestige matters.)

Ranking what's left — the Priority Shelf

Once the octopus holds only authentic, current-you yearnings, rank them. The fewer you put up top, the more likely those will thrive.

Auditing the Reality Box: the current game board

To know what's actually reachable, study the current game board of a field — its real rules, players, and loopholes now, not in your uncle's era. Then estimate the distance from where you are (point A) to your "success star," and your speed of covering it:

Stop planning the tunnel. Plan the next dot.

The old model: a career is a 40-year tunnel you commit to up front. The truth: it's "a massive, rapidly changing science laboratory" — a series of dots (jobs/phases), each a hypothesis test that teaches you about yourself and the world.

Iterate with three loops

After each dot, revise: the Want-Box loop (did my yearnings change?), the Reality-Box loop (what did I learn about what's achievable?), and occasionally the mission loop (rethink the whole direction). And mind the inertia spectrum — "cement feet" (too jump-shy) carry more regret risk than "wing feet."

The takeaway

Be a chef. Make the Want Box and Reality Box honest. Throw out the recipe, run experiments, and let each dot teach you the next one.
