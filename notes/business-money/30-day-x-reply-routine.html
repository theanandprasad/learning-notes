The 30-Day X Routine That Took Me From 850 to 1,400 Followers and 4.9M Views

By Tarang Agarwal (@tarang8811) • Published Apr 13, 2026 • Saved Jun 8, 2026
Audience Growth X / Twitter Replies Distribution

Originals build your brand. Replies build your reach. The whole playbook is one thing, not ten — show up in the comment sections where attention already lives.

ℹ️ Captured as-is. An X-growth playbook; the follower/impression/payout figures (850→1,400 followers, ~4.9M impressions, a 570K-view reply, monetization thresholds) are the author's own claims, and X's algorithm/payout rules change fast. Archived for the method, not as guaranteed results.

The one move

Get into the replies under bigger accounts in your niche. Their comment sections are a free audition stage — a top reply borrows their audience and puts you in front of people who already care about your topic. Originals = brand. Replies = reach.

The one insight

Nobody cares about your originals until they already know who you are. The fastest way to get known is to show up where attention is already pooled — the replies under accounts bigger than you. The author's single best reply pulled roughly 570K views, more than three months of his own original posts combined.

The game isn't writing better tweets. It's writing in better rooms.

Who to reply to

The sweet spot is accounts with 30K–500K followers in your niche — not Elon or MrBeast (you'll be invisible in those comment sections), and not tiny accounts with no reach. Look for ones posting 1–2× a day (later 3–5×) with a consistent reply section of recurring faces.

The timing myth

The old "reply within 30 minutes" rule no longer holds. Posts now peak at unpredictable times across a 6–24 hour window. The practical aim is to catch a tweet within roughly 3–6 hours so you're present when it crests — but what actually compounds is consistency plus niche targeting, not raw speed.

His 500K-view reply landed not because it was early, but because it was there at the peak. Being present when a post takes off beats being first.

The reply format

Every reply should pass the scroll test: if a stranger reads only your reply with no context, does it still make them think? Treat it as a standalone micro-post that happens to sit under someone else's tweet — 3–4 lines max.

The daily routine

About two hours a day, spread out — not one block. The mantra: two originals, twenty replies.

BlockTimeWhat you do
Morning ~30 min Queue 2 originals — one casual story/question, one value/contrarian take with a number. Reply to any early posts from your targets.

(3–5 min chunks)		 Notifications on for your target accounts. Ship 15–25 quality replies across the day in short bursts.
Evening ~20 min Post a PM value tweet. Reply to everyone who replied to you within the hour.

What he stopped doing

  • Scrolling For You for inspiration — it's a slot machine, not a workspace.
  • Posting threads before having an audience — a megaphone into an empty room.
  • Chasing viral trends outside his positioning — reach with no relevance.
  • Writing long originals in week 1 — short tests teach you what lands faster.
  • Following back everyone.
  • Replying to viral accounts outside his niche — it confuses the algorithm about who you're for.

Creator-payout changes to know

  1. Impressions alone no longer pay. Optimize for replies, bookmarks, and engagement from verified users.
  2. Engagement rate is a gate. Below ~1% the algorithm throttles you — his ER fell from 5% to 1.1% while chasing impressions.
  3. Monetization requires 500+ followers, verified status, and 5M impressions over 3 months.
  4. Replies to verified accounts count more than replies to non-verified ones.
  5. X is shifting toward original content. The reply strategy can get you past the impression threshold, but payouts from replies are now ~$0 — original content is "king."

Takeaway: the reply playbook still works to build an audience and cross the monetization line — but it won't pay like it used to. Pivot to originals once you're across.

The honest part

4.9M impressions didn't make him rich, didn't ship his product, and didn't close a customer. What it bought was a real audience of founders who now pay attention. Run the routine because you have something real to say — not for the dopamine hit of a big number.
