Your Org Structure Is My Opportunity

By Nikunj Kothari (@nikunj, FPV Ventures) • Published Feb 19, 2026 • Saved Jun 9, 2026
AI-Native Org Design Hiring Startups vs Incumbents Strategy

AI didn't just give everyone an assistant — it erased the skill constraint org charts were built around. Most companies bolted AI onto the old structure and called it a strategy. To a startup, that unchanged structure is the opening.

⚠️ An investor's opinion piece (FPV Ventures); the founder/exec/banker anecdotes are the author's own. Captured as a genuine strategy argument, not a how-to.

The thesis

Roles like "PM has the idea, engineer builds it" existed because you literally couldn't do the next step yourself — a real skill constraint. AI removed the constraint but nobody redrew the org chart. The companies that restructure (one person, idea→shipped, no handoffs) beat the ones that just hand everyone a copilot and leave the boxes in place. Every handoff leaks context; the gaps add up to weeks — and in those weeks a competitor is already talking to your customers.

Two conversations that frame it

The founder

His hardest problem isn't product or customers — it's hiring. He can't find people who do more than one thing well. Why: a generation spent the last decade inside companies that trained them to do exactly one job, in one box, on one org chart.

The exec

Proud of his AI rollout — copilots everywhere, adoption dashboards, 20 minutes of "efficiency gains." But his hiring plan hadn't changed, headcount was still rising, and he couldn't name a single metric that moved. He gave everyone an assistant and left the structure exactly where it was.

Symptoms of the unchanged org

The tells that you've bolted AI on without redrawing anything:

  • Same dependencies between product and design; same review cycles between design and eng.
  • Same eight-person meetings "so everyone has context."
  • Product still says "prioritize." Eng still says "capacity." The board wants Q2 projections and something about "product velocity."
  • Everyone's busy — they look around the room and it feels like progress.

The constraint that justified the boxes is gone

What fast-growing startups hire now: tinkerers

No fast-growing startup he knows is hiring "just a PM" or "just a marketer" anymore. They want tinkerers: the person who talks to the customer in the morning, builds the fix by noon, and ships it before dinner. One brain holding the whole problem. Not because they can't afford to specialize — because every time work changes hands, context leaks, and they learned to just not have that problem.

Follow the biggest line item

The Chief AI Officer tell

Every Fortune 500 is hiring a Chief AI Officer right now — the same instinct as "VP of Innovation" a decade ago. The move is to create a role so the rest of the org can stay exactly as it is: outsource the conviction instead of building it.

Tobi Lutke doesn't have a CAIO. He is the CAIO. His conviction comes from building, not briefings.

Most CEOs would rather hire someone to hold that conviction on their behalf — and it shows.

Why this is different from "startups are just faster"

Startups have always been faster — that's not news. What changed: AI lets every person on a team take something from idea to shipped product without needing anyone else to touch it. Per-seat incumbents have the exact same tools on every laptop — and a structure that forces the separation anyway.

The closing line

Your company still has a role for every function and a gap between each one. Those gaps add up to weeks, and in those weeks your customers are already talking to someone who doesn't work that way. "The pot keeps boiling."
