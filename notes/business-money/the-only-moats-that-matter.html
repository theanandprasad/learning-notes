The Only Moats That Matter

By Michael Bloch (@michaelxbloch, Quiet Capital) • Published Mar 28, 2026 • Saved Jun 8, 2026
Moats AI Strategy Defensibility Investing

In a world where AI can build any software, replicate any product, and automate any process — what actually makes a business defensible? Most answers are bets that intelligence won't get 100× faster, better, and cheaper. It will.

The one filter

Defensibility comes from two things: things that are hard to do and things that are hard to get. AI is making "hard to do" worthless. What endures is "hard to get" — because "AI compresses the time it takes to do things. It does not compress the time it takes for things to happen." Five moats pass that test.

The five moats that pass

1 · Compounding proprietary data. Not static datasets (those get synthesized or worked around — "hard to do, not hard to get"). The moat is living data: proprietary information continuously generated through operations that are themselves defensible.Orchard AI mounts cameras on farm equipment, tracking billions of fruit across millions of trees over many seasons and geographies — every pass makes the dataset richer. You can't replicate it without driving the same cameras through the same orchards for years.
2 · Network effects. Every user makes the product more valuable for every other user.DoorDash: clone the app overnight, but the drivers, restaurants, customers, and delivery density across 10,000 cities don't come with it. (Cache, an exchange fund: a larger pool → better diversification → more participants.) AI makes the cold-start problem harder — a hundred well-built clones now fight to bootstrap the same network; whoever already has liquidity compounds.
3 · Regulatory permission. Governments move at the speed of politics, not technology.Anduril needs procurement clearances and classified contracts. A bank charter takes years; FDA approval takes years. The surface area of regulation is expanding, not shrinking, as AI raises the stakes. The forms may change; the need for human permission won't.
4 · Capital at scale. The one almost everyone is underweighting — "the endgame is physical."Chip fabs, nuclear plants, and satellite constellations each cost billions. (There's a reason Musk is raising ~$75B and IPOing SpaceX even while saying money might not matter in 15 years.) When the bottleneck shifts from software to atoms, the ability to finance and deploy capital at massive scale is a defining advantage — and capital access is also institutional trust, track record, and relationships built over decades.
5 · Physical infrastructure. Factories, power plants, battery networks, data centers.Base Power is deploying thousands of battery units across Texas homes while building its own manufacturing. You can design the system in a week with AI; you cannot manufacture, install, and interconnect thousands of units in a week. Physics sets a floor that intelligence can't break — whoever broke ground first leads more every month.

The meta-moat: time that can't be parallelized

Every one of the five shares a property: they required years of real-world elapsed time to accumulate, and no amount of intelligence compresses that clock.

Two open questions the author flags

  • Trust as a moat? Someone must be accountable when things go wrong — the institution that bears the liability might become more valuable as AI does more of the work.
  • Human attention as a moat? When the cost of creating content drops to zero, the brands that already hold attention may have the most compounding advantage of all.

He believes both are real — unsure yet whether they're their own categories or byproducts of the five.

The takeaway

Hard to do, or hard to get? If your moat is bottlenecked by intelligence, you're on borrowed time. If it's bottlenecked by years, by human behavior, by physics, by political will, by capital — you're probably building something that lasts.
