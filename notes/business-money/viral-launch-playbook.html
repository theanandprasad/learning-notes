The thesis

"Launches don't fail because the product is bad. They fail because nobody saw it." Going viral and #1 Trending is engineered "from the first frame to the last repost" — a repeatable 6-step playbook built around how X's algorithm actually distributes content. The last step (the founder replying for 48 hours) is the most important.

The 6-step playbook

1 · Make a video worth stopping for. The hook is the biggest viral signal — you have 3 seconds to stop the doom-scroll. Two rules: it's worked before (10M+ views on Reels/TikTok) and it's a pattern interrupt. Then: bold claim in the first 5 seconds, product demo by second 10, shown working in real time / present tense (not "we built a tool that…"). Audio and visual must match 1:1; write at a fifth-grade reading level. (They build swipe files with 1of10 + foreplay; for @arcads_ai they mashed 5 viral hooks into one intro → 4M+ views.)
2 · Work with the right influencers. X's algorithm runs on SimClusters — 145,000+ communities; you want to "infiltrate" yours and lead it for a day. Their formula: Engagement + Audience Overlap / Cultural Impact = Success (tool: favikon). Two buckets — ICP accounts (potential customers / people in your buyer's world — where you drive sales) and Amplification accounts (news/media/high-reach — make it feel like a cultural moment). Several large credible accounts posting in a short window signals "big news" → X pushes it → you trend. (Avoid the rookie move of sorting by follower count and buying botted pages.)
3 · Write every influencer post by hand. The best creators are busy and will half-ass an AI post in 30 seconds. Pre-write everything, tailored to each creator's tone/style/POV (long threads vs memes), so it feels organic. Require the paid-partner badge. And "plant a subtle edge" — something mildly controversial a specific community can't resist arguing about, so they distribute your content while complaining about it (quote tweets, screenshots, threads = engagement). "You're not trying to get cancelled… you're engineering a reaction from a community that can't help themselves."
4 · Use the company network. Everyone overlooks this. Customers, partners, friends — every employee has hundreds of willing connections. Make it easy and systematize with incentives. Best result: one company offered the team an extra PTO day if they hit 1,000 comments in the first 24 hours — everyone DM'd their contacts, they hit it, and the launch hit 2.9M views.
5 · Include a valuable giveaway. Share something nobody else can access — your hiring system, how you close enterprise deals, your fundraising strategy. Most companies avoid it as "cheap/spammy." It isn't — "people on X love free stuff," and they'll love you for it.
6 · Track everything — the "War Room." Every impression, comment, repost, quote tweet — even comments on reposts — monitored in real time on launch day ("like a live broadcast"; 40+ people touch each launch). If an influencer post outperforms, push more engagement to it; if a thread generates heat, feed it; if the narrative drifts, course-correct. "Most teams post and pray."

Why it works: how X actually decides what goes viral

The first 30–60 minutes decide everything. X gives every post a small test audience and measures engagement velocity (replies, retweets, quote tweets, bookmarks relative to time). High ratio → pushed to a massively larger audience. Low ratio → "the post dies. It's so over."

The non-negotiable: the founder replies for 48 hours

"The founder has to reply to everything. Every single comment. Not just the first hour. For 48 hours straight." The algorithm watches whether the author is engaged, and every founder reply spawns a new reply chain. "Clear your calendar — your only job is replying and hitting up your connections." Launches where founders go all-in on replies outperform by 2–3×, and posts keep gaining traction for a full week.

Launches don't fail because the product is bad. They fail because nobody saw it.
