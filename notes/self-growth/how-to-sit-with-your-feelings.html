How to Actually Sit With Your Feelings

By Farah (Voilà Farah) · Published Aug 20, 2025 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Emotions Self-Trust Mindfulness Mental Health

"Just feel it and let it go"… but how? The difference between people who move past things and people who stay stuck is often that the first group stops delaying the feeling — they feel it, then they act.

The core idea

There's a small, repeatable routine you can run whenever an ambiguous feeling shows up: Name the feeling → Feel it in your body → Sit with it → Take action (or let it go), then repeat for whatever surfaces next.

The insight underneath it: taking days or weeks to "decide how you feel" is usually avoidance dressed up as processing. Actually feeling the thing and then acting on it — while staying detached from the outcome — is what signals self-trust.

The friend who got over things instantly

The author was once close friends with a girl she admired for one specific thing: she could get over almost anything almost instantly. Where the author would take days, sometimes weeks, to "decide how she felt," the friend processed in real time — decided how she felt, and acted on it at once.

For a long time this confused her. Was the friend really processing, or just acting impulsively? The answer came on an ordinary afternoon: the two of them sitting in a park on a sunny Vancouver summer day, talking about love lives and weekend plans. The friend had been seeing a guy who kept giving mixed signals. They traded perspectives, and then the friend simply made up her mind — and stuck with it.

"What if we're wrong?"
"Then I'll do something else."

That detachment from the outcome surprised the author. It wasn't recklessness; it was self-trust — a quiet confidence that whatever happened, she could handle the next step. And that's when it clicked:

It's not that I need more days to feel my feelings; usually it's that I'm delaying feeling them — and delaying taking action.— the realisation in the park

The 4-step routine

What the friend did intuitively can be run deliberately as a loop. Whenever a murky feeling arrives, walk it through these four steps — and when new feelings surface (they will), run them through the same loop.

1Name the feeling

In her early therapy sessions, when asked how she felt, the author's whole vocabulary was about three words: happy, sad, angry. Most people are the same. But there are far more feelings than three, and putting the right word to one is a powerful starting place.

This is what a "feelings wheel" is for — a tool that helps you narrow a vague feeling down to something specific. You start from a broad core feeling and follow it outward to a more precise one. And you can land on a mix; you're rarely feeling just one thing.

2Feel it in your body

Once you've narrowed the feeling (or feelings) down, the next move is to stop thinking about the feeling and instead feel it physically. Where in your body does it show up? Is your chest sinking? Is it sitting in your gut? Do your arms feel heavier?

Take the work example: sad → hurt → embarrassed about a mistake. Forget the label for a moment and pay attention to the body's reaction instead. For the author this was close to life-changing, because she almost never noticed her body — her instinct was to rationalize, intellectualize, and think about a feeling until she'd forgotten the body entirely.

3Sit with it

Then simply stay there. The first few times, this can be uncomfortable — and if it is, sit with that discomfort too. The point isn't to fix or flee the sensation; it's to let yourself be in it long enough that it stops running the show from underneath.

4Take action

The author's favourite part. Once you've named, felt, and sat with something, there are two broad ways to respond — one that keeps you stuck, and one that moves you. Take the example of a co-worker whose dirty looks you've come to dread when you walk in: it makes you angry → aggressive → hostile.

Avoid taking action

  • Ruminating on it
  • Asking why it happened this way instead of that
  • Constantly feeling sorry for yourself
  • Slowly building resentment

This looks like processing, but it's an avoidance trap.

Take action

  • Talk to her about it directly
  • Genuinely decide not to care — only if you're not ruminating
  • Find other solutions

Each of these closes the loop instead of looping forever.

The catch with "deciding not to care" is the honesty test: it only counts as action if you've actually stopped ruminating. Otherwise it's just avoidance with a calmer face.

It's a habit

None of this is a one-time fix. You run the loop again whenever another feeling arrives — and it takes consistency and conscious effort before it becomes second nature. It's a habit, and like any habit it takes time. But it's far more productive than overthinking your way to nowhere.

Expect the actions you take to bring up secondary and tertiary feelings of their own. That's normal — you handle those the same way, looping them back through the four steps. And the more you practice, the more you build the thing underneath it all: self-trust, the same quiet confidence the friend in the park had, which is what lets you navigate more complex feelings and genuinely hard times.
