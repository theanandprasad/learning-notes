The Operator Trap

By Chris Williamson (@ChrisWillx) • Published Jun 15, 2026 • Saved Jun 15, 2026
Busyness vs Effectiveness Leverage Self-Worth Productivity

There's a painful transition everyone eventually has to make: from operator guy to idea guy. The trap is that you spent your whole career becoming brilliant at the thing you now need to stop doing.

The core idea

Early on, your only edge is work rate — no experience to draw on, talent capped by inexperience, so you just outwork everyone: emails, calls, invoices, copy, contractors. "It's all you." But that stage expires. The problem is you've built "a monster which sucks in difficult, tedious tasks and spits out completed efforts" — and wired a link between being busy and being successful. Letting go is hard precisely because being busy feels better than being effective.

Your job isn't to work hard, your job is to have great ideas.— Joe Hudson

Operator vs idea guy

Why busyness is so sticky

It's easy to count the emails you sent or the hours you spent on calls; it's hard to know whether any of it moved you toward what you actually want. So we default to the measurable.

Being busy is more satisfying than being effective.

Williamson lays out four reasons the operator habit refuses to die:

It's a dopamine fix. Checking the Done Box feels like completing something — even when it wasn't a great use of the day. "At least you don't feel useless, and you didn't have any time to consider that you might not be fully actualising your potential anyway."
It makes you feel important. "A full calendar is a hedge against existential loneliness." The unspoken monologue: "Look at how many calls I have today! Look at all the people who need my time… I must be important. I must be valuable."
It's socially rewarded. Conspicuous busyness beats quiet effectiveness in the eyes of others. "Even if the best thing for your mission was for you to go lie on the beach and think today, who is going to congratulate you for that?" Near-burnout is worn like a badge of honour. "Obvious productivity is more praised than private efficacy."
Your self-worth depends on it. So much of your sense of worth comes from overcoming hard things and pushing through tasks you don't want to do — so if you actually reach the goal of "not having to do anything I don't want to," where does satisfaction come from now?

The cruel paradox: "almost everyone's life goal is 'I just don't have to do anything I don't want to do'" — and yet that's exactly the state we find hardest to allow ourselves, because the struggle was the source of our worth.

Why we can't savour the win

And even when we succeed, we can't enjoy it — because we're "constantly peering over the shoulder of the present moment to see what's coming next." Mid-achievement, you're already moving the goalposts further out.

We are all chasing a sense of completion but we never actually allow ourselves to savour any tastes of completion that we get along the way.
