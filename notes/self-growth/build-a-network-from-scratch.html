Stop Waiting to Meet the "Right" People — Build a Network From Scratch

By Lindsay Casale (@LindsayCasale) · Published Mar 5, 2026 · Saved Jun 9, 2026
Networking Relationships Career Long Game

Building a network isn't about being an extrovert or already knowing the right people. It's about intentionality, showing up consistently, and playing the long game — anyone can do it from zero.

The core sequence

Get in the room (online and in person) → engage genuinely before you need anything → use warm, friend-of-a-friend intros → then nurture those relationships over time. Never lead with the ask before the relationship exists. Networks compound: the value of what you build shows up later, often in directions you can't predict.

Get yourself in a room

When the author moved to San Francisco with no network at all, she made one rule for herself: commit to one event per week. She found them on Eventbrite and Luma, searching for meetups around marketing, PR, and business. Even now, she still recommends roughly one in-person event a month, year-round — proximity to people is the raw input the whole system runs on.

If you're introverted and the room feels intimidating, four moves make it survivable:

Bring a friend as a "home base." Having a familiar face lowers the activation energy — but don't cling to them all night. Use them as a place to reset, not a place to hide.
Approach people who are also alone. Someone standing by themselves is almost always relieved to be talked to. It's the lowest-risk opening in the room.
Set a meeting goal. Give yourself a concrete target — e.g. "meet 3 useful new people" — so you have a finish line instead of an open-ended ordeal.
Introduce yourself to the organizer. They know everyone and want their event to go well. Tell them what you're looking for and ask them to introduce you.

The PR trick for online networking

The author's PR background gives her the governing rule for reaching people online:

You have to earn the right to pitch.— Lindsay Casale, on borrowing the PR mindset for networking

You don't earn that right with a cold DM. You earn it by becoming a name they recognize before you ever message them. Follow the people you want to know, turn on notifications, and comment and engage genuinely and consistently — until your name starts to feel familiar to them. Only then do you send the DM.

And when you do, keep it short and honest. Her sample DM, paraphrased:

I've followed your work for a while and really admire it. Would you be open to connecting? I'll be upfront — I'm hoping to learn about X — and in return I'd be glad to help with Y. If now's not a good time, no worries at all.— paraphrased sample DM

Acknowledge their work, ask permission, be upfront about your intentions, and offer your own value. Keep it concise. If they don't reply, let it go and move on — no follow-up guilt-trips.

Work the friend-of-a-friend layer

Your existing connections are a hidden directory. Ask them plainly: "Do you know anyone at X company / in this role / in this industry?" A warm intro from someone you both know beats a cold approach every time.

Once you get one, handle it well:

  • Start low-pressure. A DM or email, then a quick informal call — nothing heavy.
  • Come with curiosity. Ask about their path and their work; you're there to learn, not to extract.
  • Treat it like they might be interviewing you. Be prepared and respectful of their time — first impressions still count, even in a casual chat.
  • Close with a clear, specific ask. Only at the end, and only something concrete: "Would you mind giving my resume a look?" or "If you hear of openings, would you send them my way / refer me?"

The order matters as much as the content. Never lead with the ask. The relationship comes first; the request comes last.

Cultivate what you build

A network isn't a one-time harvest; it's a garden. Relationships are maintained over time through small, low-cost touches:

  • Occasional check-ins — "saw this, thought of you" with a relevant link or article.
  • Coffee or a catch-up every few months, with no agenda attached.
  • Congratulating people on milestones — new jobs, launches, wins.

The key is to not be transactional. You're not banking favors; you're keeping a relationship alive. Value shows up unpredictably and on its own schedule. Her own example: a casual, no-agenda catch-up with a boss from five years earlier led to her being hired by that person months later — a payoff she could never have planned or rushed.

Final thoughts

None of this is about collecting contacts or padding a follower count. It's about building genuine relationships that grow in directions you can't foresee. It feels slow at first — you put in effort and see little — but networks compound, and the early investment keeps paying out for years.

It's not about who you know today. It's about who you'll be glad you stayed in touch with five years from now.— paraphrasing the closing idea
