How to Become Dangerously Focused

By Tim Denning (@Tim_Denning) • Published Jun 18, 2026 • Saved Jun 18, 2026
Focus Priorities Discipline Phone Detox

We're living through a focus epidemic — brain cells lit on fire by choice. The antidote is extreme, because the problem is more extreme than people will admit. Get focus back and you become "dangerous": you have no competition.

⚠️ Provocative manifesto, captured as-is. A deliberately extreme, profane, personal-anecdote-driven essay; the screen-time figures and routines are the author's own claims. Archived as one practitioner's hard-line method, not a prescription. Ends with a self-promotional CTA.

The core move

A scatterbrain is really someone with no priorities — they drift from one urgent task to the next, and "whatever is loudest dominates the calendar." The fix is to choose a single priority and defend it with almost inhuman discipline. "Focused people are dangerous because they have no competition. It's life on easy mode, and it's the best cheat code I know."

2 · Tie focus to survival

A goal is a fantasy. Visions are p0rn. Systems are idealistic.

None of the standard self-help tools work — "if they did, humans wouldn't be at peak burnout, peak loneliness, and living through a meaning crisis." The workaround: tie what you focus on directly to your survival. Denning keeps a photo of his two daughters beside him and imagines they'll starve if the essay doesn't get done. It's a deliberate delusion — and in 3 years (since his first daughter was born) he's never missed writing an essay.

"Your survival is deeply motivating. It helps your priorities transcend your own existence. And when a priority is about more than just you, it's easier to focus." Tell yourself you'll ruin your life if this single priority doesn't work out.

4 · Stop dabbling like an amateur

Because you can't know the outcome of any goal in advance, obsessing over knowing how it'll turn out makes you hedge your bets and dabble — never investing enough to reach the outcome.

Dangerously focused people don't dabble. They assume things will work out. They go all in.

Going all in means committing to one project, pouring all your energy into it, and choosing obsession over lukewarm passion — okay with failure as the price of finding out.

5 · Extend your flow states

Denning credits Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's Flow. The protocol: remove distractions, focus on one task, make the work slightly beyond your capabilities, drink coffee, and do work that gives you energy — "an hour in flow will feel like 5 minutes." Most people's flow lasts an hour or two; he extends his to 4–8 hours (longest ever: 16 hours straight). "Longer flow states are more potent." The two requirements: a distraction-free environment and a free calendar.

7 · Let some crazy bastards into your life

The bar around you is so low that focusing for 30 minutes earns compliments — which does more harm than good. Get around people who hold you to a far higher standard. Denning learned it from Olympic swimmer Kieren Perkins, who explained how he trained hard enough to win:

It's hard to skip a 4 AM training session when you've got three other Olympic gold medalist teammates to drag you out of bed if you dare try to skip a session.— Kieren Perkins

"You lie to yourself when you only hear your own voice. Outside voices wake you the hell up from being in love with yourself and thinking you're focused."

8 · Cultivate a psychopathic sense of urgency

A lack of focus needs consequences — and most people have none, so nothing bad happens when they slack. Hardwire urgency instead. "'I'll start in 3 months' is ruining your life." Circumstances won't be better; there's always chaos, so you work around it (or learn to thrive in it). The "now" must be evidence-based. Denning's own pattern interrupt, after a 2015 cancer near-miss:

What the hell makes you think you have the extreme privilege of being alive in three months to start this goal then?

"Believing you'll be alive beyond today is sheer arrogance… You could be dead tomorrow. So do it today or shut up and stop complaining."

Bringing it together

It's simple but not easy. "Unless there's a reason for extreme focus, you'll default to a distracted state which produces low states of consciousness and piss-poor results." We're at war — guard your focus, or face the regret of not getting what you knew you could.

Be honest — are you actually focused, or just busy?
