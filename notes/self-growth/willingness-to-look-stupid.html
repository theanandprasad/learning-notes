Willingness to Look Stupid

By Dan Luu • Published danluu.com (undated) • Saved Jun 9, 2026
Learning Ego Mindset Asking Questions Contrarian

People frequently think Dan Luu is stupid — because he doesn't mind looking stupid, so he doesn't adjust his behavior to avoid it. The compounding upside of that willingness, he argues, vastly outweighs the downside.

The core claim

Most people quietly optimize to avoid looking (or feeling) stupid. That instinct blocks the very things that build deep understanding and find high-ROI ideas: asking naive questions, owning failures, trying obviously-dumb-sounding things, and pursuing ideas everyone else dismissed out of hand. The cost of looking stupid is small and momentary; the compounding benefit of being willing to is large.

Where it started: the "stupid" questions

In college, a group who were themselves struggling with the material concluded Dan was "really dumb" — because he asked stupid-sounding questions. The questions sounded stupid precisely because he'd already seen past the obvious-but-wrong answer and was probing for the subtler real one.

The payoff is tiny in any single instance but compounds enormously over time. Dan notes that people with genuinely deep understanding of a topic still ask naive-sounding questions — it's the same habit that got them there in the first place.

The video-game tell: excuses vs. improvement

Playing competitive games as a teen, he could watch everyone improve (or not) over time. The pattern was stark: people who blamed external factors when they lost almost never got good.

It shows up everywhere

Most of the essay is a catalogue of real instances where Dan accepted looking stupid — and usually came out ahead. A selection:

COVID, early. Bought a half-mask respirator on 2020-01-26 (using N95s the week before) based on existing SARS contact-tracing and air-filtration literature. Mocked for months. The person he lived with found masking on an empty BART too embarrassing — caught COVID early, then got long COVID for months.
Chip-verification, pre-tape-out. The head of verification wanted to re-run old "golden" tests (almost no chance of finding bugs) instead of the best test mix — explicitly so the team wouldn't look incompetent if an old test later caught something. Dan was shocked someone would knowingly do the wrong thing to avoid looking stupid. He's since seen most people do exactly that — and considers it self-sabotage.
Air filters since 2012. Called paranoid for years; added capacity in a wildfire-risk area. Was essentially unaffected through two severe wildfire seasons while others developed lasting issues (in one case, persistent asthma).
Asking questions. A top failure mode in junior engineers: too afraid to look stupid to ask, so they learn slowly — sometimes badly enough to be PIP'd and fired. It can take him up to six weeks to convince a new hire it's genuinely okay to ask. (If your workplace punishes questions, leave if you can.)
Deliberate practice at the edge. Repeating an easy climbing move many ways to feel what works; wanting to set up cones to learn exactly where the edge of a car is. Strangers literally point and laugh — it's how he improves quickly.
Medical care. Doctors get confident and condescending fast. In the most extreme case he'd have died if he'd listened; in the next, gone blind. For blood draws he states what works; the ~half of staff who take him seriously get a clean draw, the half who treat him like an idiot fail and/or leave a hematoma.
This website / long posts. Designers and typographers tell him the site is stupid — but the current design tested better on engagement and sharing, and the blog out-traffics most sub-$100B corporate blogs. He writes 5,000–10,000-word posts against the "keep it to 500–1,000 words" advice.
Career & recruiting. Quitting Centaur to attend RC and switch into software (seen as very stupid in 2013); taking his current job (almost everyone thought it dumb). His recruiting pitch leads with the downsides — sounds comically stupid, yet he's never failed to close a full-time candidate.

Other examples in the essay: buying the laptop that ships in the smallest box; preferring Moderna over Pfizer; reframing "feeling dumb" as "the feeling of learning"; car insurance with no own-damage coverage; being earnest / a "tryhard"; and posting on the internet at all.

Why it's a competitive edge at work

Beyond faster learning, willingness to look stupid is where the high-ROI ideas live.

The upside/downside ledger

For anything not socially judged — winning a game, understanding something, building things — it's all upside. The downside is concentrated in things that are about social judgment, like dates and interviews. And even there, Dan reframes much of it as upside:

  • Dates: if asking what a word means makes a date visibly think you're stupid, that's strong early evidence of incompatibility — better learned sooner.
  • Interviews: the genuine hard case. At big companies the interviewer may have no bearing on your eventual work life, so the cost is real. But the habit of saying what he believes true is too deeply ingrained — and too valuable elsewhere — to rewire just for interviews.

"But I'm already willing to look stupid"

Dan expects most readers to conclude they're already great at this — the same way most people, asked, believe they're creative, innovative, and big risk-takers. (He knows one of the most conservative people alive who counts "sometimes jaywalking" as taking big risks.) A real check: he runs into someone obviously thinking he's an idiot about once a week.

If that essentially never happens to you, he suggests at least one of these is true:

Filtered world
you only interact with hand-picked people, screened of these reactions
No exposure
you don't post to the internet (or don't read the comments)
Avoiding it
you're actively steering clear of looking stupid
Not noticing
it's happening and you don't see it (he thinks this one's least likely — people work hard to make sure you know)

The deeper trap isn't filtering out dumb-sounding comments consciously — that's fine if it's low-overhead. It's having an unconscious process that refuses to even evaluate a stupid-sounding thought, in others or in your own head. Note he doesn't court weirdness: he conforms cheaply where it doesn't matter (dressing casually on the west coast) to spend his "weirdness points" on radical ideas, not on appearances.

The catch, and the contexts

Stupid-sounding thoughts are frequently wrong. If you won't lean on social proof to filter them, you have to hone your own intuition or find trusted friends who can catch the ones that are actually stupid — and since almost no one tries, you can get relatively good at it just by trying at all.

He's also explicit about scope: in contexts where it's more important to look competent than to be competent, this advice doesn't apply (he cites Ali Partovi's story of honesty costing his company a Yahoo acquisition — which indirectly led to Paul Graham founding YC). Dan has deliberately avoided such contexts, though he grants they're probably more lucrative.

"Often, the dumb solution is something any idiot could've come up with and the reason the problem hasn't been solved is because no one was willing to think the dumb thought until an idiot like me looked at the problem."

On "how" to stop caring: Dan says it's idiosyncratic, so general advice fails. For him it came easily — he genuinely felt stupid growing up (smarter childhood friends; an abusive household where he was berated for hours for being "stupid"), so a stranger's low opinion is water off a duck's back. A path he wouldn't recommend, but which worked out okay.
