The Mathematical Reason Most People Never "Make It"

By Kaguura Gichuru (The Write Path) • Published Jan 18, 2026 • Saved Jun 15, 2026
Price's Law Power Laws Leverage Explore / Exploit

Effort doesn't equal outcome — not in any complex system. The square root of the people do half the work, everywhere you look. Once you see Price's Law, you can stop treading water and start letting the math work for you.

Price's Law

The square root of the number of people in a domain does 50% of the work. Discovered by physicist Derek J. de Solla Price (1963) studying why a few researchers dominated their fields — the distribution wasn't a bell curve, it was a power law. The rest still publish/post/work; their output just barely registers.

√n = your high performers  (n = total population)

100 employees → 10 produce half the output · 10,000 scientists → 100 produce half the research · a team of 25 → 5 carry it.

Once you see it, you can't unsee it

Competence is rarer than we pretend

Competence scales linearly and incompetence scales exponentially.

Price's Law violates our egalitarian instincts — we want to believe effort equals outcome and hard work is the great equalizer. Real competence (the kind that ships products and moves needles) needs four ingredients compounding in the same direction. Most people have one or two. The square root has all four.

Two psychological traps

If you ARE the system: three applications

For a one-person business or personal brand, Price's Law has immediate, practical consequences.

1. Content — √n of your posts drive 50% of results

The author's own 8 months: ~30 newsletters, 700+ notes, thousands of comments → only ~15 notes and 2 newsletters went viral and drove an overwhelming share of subscribers. That's 17 of 730+ outputs ≈ 2.3% driving the majority of growth — "but I never would have found those 17 without posting the other 713." Publish 100 posts, ~10 matter; publish 400, ~20 matter; the rest are practice rounds. You cannot predict which will hit before you publish.

Produce volume early. You need enough shots on goal to identify patterns.
Track everything — shares, replies, clicks, sign-ups, sales. Avoid vanity metrics; look for business outcomes.
Identify your √n. After 50–100 pieces, patterns emerge. For him it was "long-form storytelling with a contrarian hook." For you, maybe tactical how-tos, vulnerability, or data breakdowns.
Double down ruthlessly. Make more of what works; kill everything else. It feels limiting — but the other 90% wasn't mattering anyway.

Result he reports: "I now publish 70% less than I did a year ago. My engagement is up 4x."

2. Skills / Offer — √n of your skills drive half your value

You have dozens of skills; a handful are your multipliers. Stop chasing "well-rounded" — the education system, the corporate ladder, and the self-help industrial complex all oversell balanced competence ("work on your weaknesses!"). Instead, get so good at your two or three multiplier skills that you're top 1% at the combination.

You don't need to be the best writer in the world… But if you're top 10% at writing and top 10% at strategy, you're suddenly top 1% at strategic writing. That's a great market position.

3. Time Management — ~6 of your 40 hours actually matter

If you work 40 hours a week, about 6 of them move needles; the other 34 are maintenance, busywork, and meetings-that-could've-been-emails. His method: track time in 2-hour blocks (15-min increments are too granular), each labeled high-leverage (writing, strategy, client calls) or low-leverage (admin, email, tinkering).

Not Ferriss's 4-hour week ("a bit far-fetched for someone still building") — but a 40-hour week with a guaranteed 6 hours of needle-moving work is doable. "The new meta."

The paradox: you still need the other 90%
