Burning All Your Bridges Is a Cheat Code to Success

By Tim Denning (@Tim_Denning) · Published Apr 16, 2026 · Saved Jun 9, 2026
Reinvention Identity Change Decisiveness

Deliberately cutting off your past — "burning your bridges" — removes the option to retreat. With no way back, the decision is made for you, and you're forced forward into a rebirth.

Note: A deliberately provocative manifesto by a creator who sells writing courses; it ends with a promo CTA and leans on personal anecdotes. Distilled and toned down here — the original is blunter. Captured for the idea, not as one-size-fits-all advice (cutting people off has real costs; use judgment).

The key idea

"Don't burn your bridges" is the conventional wisdom; Denning argues the opposite. When you can't return to a past life, the decision is made for you — the only way is forward. Burning bridges is how you accept change, shed an old identity, and let yourself be "reborn." But do it without ego, via four rules.

Why it works: it frees you from the past

It's easy to get trapped by an old identity. Denning was a banker. He was a top Medium writer right up until that era faded. The temptation in both cases was to cling to a version of himself that the world had already moved past.

His framing: "Those who win in life are reborn over and over." Removing the option to return is what sets you free to do the reinventing. Eleven years ago he put his real name on LinkedIn precisely so there'd be no taking it back — burning that bridge committed him publicly to becoming the writer he eventually became. The lack of an exit was the whole point.

It's an acceptance of change

The only people who lose, he argues, are those who refuse to change. Being able to burn a bridge is really the same muscle as being able to embrace change — you can't do one without the other.

Identity is powerful, and becoming several different people across one life is a superpower, not a failure of consistency. His own arc: drummer → musician/producer → twenty-something entrepreneur → banker → writer → father → "still figuring out what's next." "Being a chameleon means you can become anybody."

The hardest bridge

Sometimes the bridge isn't a job or a platform — it's your childhood, your hometown, or old friends. Denning describes cutting off a best friend who was struggling with addiction, along with some other connections, in order to heal and move on. He frames it as necessary self-preservation, handled with care rather than cruelty.

His observation: old connections often "hold on to the person we were decades ago instead of who we are now." Different phases of life simply need different people around you, and refusing to acknowledge that traps everyone in an outdated story.

The four rules to do it right

Burning a bridge well is not the same as torching it. The difference is ego. These four rules keep the act clean.

Rule 1 — No ego. Ego is the enemy. Don't leave with a middle-finger, "I'm-out-because-this-place-sucks" farewell post. It's a waste of time and it quietly turns off future collaborators. Humility, by contrast, tends to bring more opportunities after the breakup, not fewer.
Rule 2 — Don't explain yourself. Explaining invites argument, and argument lets people build a case for you to revert to the old you. When an angry reader demanded the "old" Tim back, his entire reply was: "People change." As he puts it, "the only person you owe answers to is yourself."
Rule 3 — It's okay to ghost. Normally ghosting is weak, but here it's a gift. Most people stop after one or two attempts, so silence spares you both the explaining and the pitch to come back. "An email, call, or text is a request — not an obligation."
Rule 4 — Avoid temptation. Right after burning a bridge, the pull to run back is strongest. Resist it. "If it didn't work before, it's not going to work again." A new path with no fallback is precisely what forces you to make the new thing work.

The end is a new beginning

Let one phase actually end so the next one can start. Make peace with the ending, embrace the chaos of the transition, and let calm take over once there's genuinely no way back. The discomfort isn't a sign you chose wrong — it's the cost of the rebirth.

"Bridges look spectacular as they burn." — Tim Denning
