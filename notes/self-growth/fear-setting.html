Fear-Setting: The Most Valuable Exercise I Do Every Month

By Tim Ferriss • Published May 15, 2017 • Saved Jun 9, 2026
Decision-Making Fear Stoicism Anxiety

Don't set goals — set fears. A Stoic-rooted, three-page exercise for the hard decisions you keep postponing: define the nightmare in detail, and it usually shrinks to something survivable and repairable.

Set aside a certain number of days, during which you shall be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough dress, saying to yourself the while: "Is this the condition that I feared?"— Seneca (Tim's epigraph)

The core idea

Borrowed from the Stoics' premeditatio malorum (premeditation of evils). We don't act on big decisions because of vague, undefined dread — and "what we fear doing most is usually what we most need to do." When you write the fear out in painstaking detail, you almost always find the worst case is survivable, often easily reversible, while the cost of inaction is the thing that quietly compounds.

The exercise: three pages

Pick one stressful decision you've been avoiding, phrase it as "What if I…", and fill three pages. Write without editing; aim for volume. Do it quarterly or monthly.

Page 2 — The upside you're ignoring

What might be the benefits of an attempt or a partial success? Now that you've defined the nightmare, list the more probable or definite positive outcomes (internal and external) — and rate their impact on a 1–10 scale.

The asymmetry is the point: worst-case downside usually scores 3–4 and is reversible; the probable upside often scores 9–10.
