Advice That Actually Worked For Me

By Nabeel S. Qureshi•Published Jul 4, 2022•Saved Jun 9, 2026
Energy Habits Thinking Productivity Self-Growth

Most self-help is generic and useless. This is the short list of things that, after actually trying them, changed how Nabeel works and thinks — split between managing your energy and sharpening your mind.

The throughline

Output is dominated by consistency, not peak performance — and consistency runs on energy. So the first job is to protect and compound your baseline energy; the second is to install thinking habits (numbers, steelmanning, writing, synthesis, logic trees) that make the energy land on true beliefs and real problems. Both halves compound: small daily wins feed tomorrow's; clear thinking feeds clearer thinking.

Nabeel groups his ten lessons into two buckets. The first four are about energy and habits — the raw fuel of getting things done. The last six are about thinking well — what you do with the fuel. We'll keep all ten, in order.

Part I — Energy & Habits

1. Maximize your baseline energy

Exercise at least five days a week. Energy compounds: if you start the morning by getting something done, your whole day operates from a higher baseline. The crucial reframe is that mental energy is not a depleting tank. Spending it on productive, important things actually gives you more energy — a positive feedback loop — whereas procrastinating, scrolling, and staying in bed drains it. The way out of an energy rut isn't to wait for motivation; it's to do something tiny (empty the dishwasher, write one sentence) for a small hit of accomplishment, and build from there.

2. Do the most important thing first thing — before social media

Your first actions set the day's spiral. Do the single most important thing before you check any social media. Even if you accomplish nothing else, you've done the one thing — and that matters more than it sounds, because most people fail from accumulating zero-output days, not from a low ceiling on their best days. The danger of social media is subtler than wasted time: it delivers a "reward" that perniciously substitutes for the reward of doing the productive thing — the same way sugar substitutes for real calories. You feel fed without being nourished.

3. Tell the right stories about yourself

The story you tell about yourself matters, and it tends to come truer with the telling. Tell yourself you're an energetic person, or that you don't get jetlagged, and — in Nabeel's experience — it has a way of becoming so.

Nabeel flags this one heavily himself: it comes with all the obvious disclaimers and isn't a license for magical thinking. He keeps it on the list only because it has actually worked for him.

4. Don't sleep too much

The sweet spot is roughly 7–7.5 hours — partly genetic, so it varies. Oversleeping costs time and may be bad for you. The weaker, safer version of the claim: there's a real difference between 7 and 9 hours, and many 9-hour sleepers can train themselves into 7-hour sleepers, then compound the recovered two hours a day into something large over a year.

Sleep is like hunger — you shouldn't just sleep whenever you happen to feel like it. — paraphrasing Alexey Guzey

Part II — Thinking Well

5. Get in the habit of Fermi estimation, looking up key quantities, and using bounds

The smartest people "think in numbers." They don't take claims at face value — they estimate orders of magnitude and sanity-check plausibility. Most people don't, which is exactly why they're so easily fooled (the viral "if Elon gave everyone $X..." memes; wildly miscalibrated COVID risk estimates).

Nabeel's example: in SBF's Conversation with Tyler, a back-of-envelope suggested a global blockchain would need on the order of ~1,000,000 transactions/sec, while Ethereum at the time did about ~10 — off by roughly six orders of magnitude. The bounding technique is the everyday version: "it's not as high as X, not as low as Y, so it's roughly Z."

6. Whenever you make a claim, imagine the strongest possible argument against it

Borrow the chess analogy. Weak players play "hope chess" — they make a move and hope the opponent replies poorly. Strong players test each move against the opponent's strongest available response. Your brain resists modeling the opponent as strong, because it wants your idea to work. Doing it anyway — steelmanning the counterargument before you commit — is essential if what you actually care about is having true beliefs.

7. Write regularly; learn to "think in writing"

This is true for everyone, whether or not you ever publish. Keep a running Google Doc and add a page a day. Writing "clears your cache" — it gets the half-formed thoughts out of your head and onto the page, which makes room for new ones to form. (Nabeel points to Holden Karnofsky's guide on this.)

8. Do a weekly review

Every Sunday, spend about an hour: what went well, what went poorly, what you're aiming for next week. The value is the mode switch — it forces you out of "doing mode" and into "reflection mode," which is where the compounding weekly improvements come from.

9. Synthesize things as you read

Reading is not the same as understanding. Your brain has to build its own encoding of the material. Understanding is closely related to compression: can you restate the idea in your own words — several different ways? Draw it. Write it the way Simple-English Wikipedia would. This doubles as a bullshit filter, in both directions: if you can't restate something, you don't actually understand it.

10. Map out problems using logic trees

Also called morphological analysis — Nabeel calls it "80% of a consultant's secret sauce." Break a problem into logically exhaustive parts, recursively, so you end up with a full map and can spot the options you'd otherwise overlook. The canonical example is profit: profit splits into revenue and costs; revenue splits into price × quantity; and so on. It's especially useful for ambiguous startup questions like "how do we grow faster?"

If you keep one thing

The energy lessons and the thinking lessons aren't separate lists — they're two ends of the same machine. Protect your baseline energy and you get consistent days; install the thinking habits and those days point at true beliefs and real problems. Both compound. Start tomorrow morning with one important thing, before the phone.
