How to Be a Great Conversationalist

By Bella Dane · Published Nov 21, 2025 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Communication Social Skills Listening Charisma

If you can master communication, you're 10x closer to any goal you have. So many of us have lost the art of good conversation — and the whole art comes down to one thing: genuine curiosity.

The core idea

Great conversation is mostly listening, genuine curiosity, and intention — not talking more. When you make the other person feel truly heard, you become the memorable one. Put all the focus on them, and the attention quietly comes back to you.

Below are the six specific things you can implement to set yourself apart.

1The more you speak, the less is retained

Let go of needing to say a lot. If you always have to give your opinion, put your two cents in, or contribute to everything, you actually lower your "contribution score" — the level of perceived value your contribution has.

If you're always talking, people glaze over when you start, because there's no way everything you say has that much value. The person who only speaks when they have something valuable is different: when they start, everyone shuts up and focuses, because they know it'll be worth hearing. That's a high contribution score.

More exclusivity = higher perceived value.

2Let them talk about themselves

If you let someone talk about themselves for 30 minutes, they'll think they're in love with you. People love to talk about themselves and rarely get the chance — so let them.

The art of good conversation is genuine curiosity, and being a good listener is a skill very few people have. They'll like you more, and you actually connect. Here's the counterintuitive part:

When you put all the focus on them, all the attention goes back on you. You become truly memorable — not by performing, but by paying attention.

3Ask more interesting + intentional questions

Most default questions are flat. Small, intentional swaps make the same exchange come alive.

Boring default

"what did you do last weekend?"

Intentional swap

"do you have any exciting plans this weekend?"

Boring default

"what do you do for work?"

Intentional swap

"do you have any exciting projects you're working on?"

Favourite career follow-ups

  • "do you like it?"
  • "what do you like about it?"
  • "if you could have any career, would it be this?"

And actually notice — reflect it back

Reflecting back what you hear beats a dead "oh cool" every time:

Lazy

"oh cool."

Noticing

"ohhh okay, so you're really passionate about X"
"you seem really interested in X"

Be intentional and notice!

4Don't go on autopilot

Someone asks "how are you" and you go "good, how are you?" — that's the most useless, boring phrase of all time. It'd genuinely be more interesting to stand there silently. Every "how are you" is an opportunity to tell them something about you.

Autopilot

"good, how are you?"

"I'm great! But I'm starving, I've heard the food here is really good — what are you going to get?"
"I'm exhausted honestly, I didn't think I was going to come, but I'm so happy I did because of X."
"I'm fantastic, I'm ready for another drink — I normally get this, what are you going to get?"
Don't forget you have a pulse when someone asks how you are.

5Pause and breathe

Take a breath before you answer a question. It shows the other person: "wow, she's really thinking about my question, she actually gives a damn."

And think before you blurt. The pause is uncommon, so when you do it, it stands out as authentic — you're not a robot on autopilot. It signals you truly want a real conversation and to connect.

The move: breath → think → answer. A single beat of silence does more for your presence than any clever line.

6Let them tell their story first

If someone is telling a story, let them tell their damn story — before you jump in with "omg same, let me tell you my story" and the whole conversation becomes about you.

Knowing someone can relate is powerful, so if you relate, relate — but let them talk first. Ask at least a few questions before you share your piece. You want them to feel safe and open, which only happens when you truly listen and give a damn.

Hijacking

"omg same, let me tell you my story" — and now it's all about you.

Holding space

Let them finish → ask a few questions → then share that you relate.
