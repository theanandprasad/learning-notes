101 Additional Advices

By Kevin Kelly (The Technium) • Published Apr 18, 2024 • Saved Jun 9, 2026
Life Advice Aphorisms Wisdom Decisions

An all-new set of unsolicited advice Kevin Kelly wrote for his 73rd birthday — a sequel to his viral "68 Bits of Unsolicited Advice," and none of these overlap with his book.

About this list

This is a curated selection of roughly 28 of Kelly's original 101 aphorisms, grouped into six themes. Each line below is reproduced verbatim, but this is only a slice — read the full list of 101 at the source. Kelly notes he keeps 460 more in his book Excellent Advice for Living (promotional plug at the end).

On starting, failing & persisting

The recurring move here: the bottleneck is almost never talent or ideas — it's beginning, and then not stopping.

  • Many fail to finish, but many more fail to start. The hardest work in any work is to start.
  • Don't fear failure. Fear average.
  • Fail fast. Fail often. Fail forward. Failing is not a disgrace if you keep failing better.
  • When you try something new, think of it as success / learning to succeed (not success / failure).
  • There is no formula for success, but two formulas for failure: not trying and not persisting.
  • The more persistent you are, the more chances you get to be lucky.
  • Slow progress is still a million times better than no progress.

On decisions & thinking

Kelly treats decisions as reversible by default and thinking as something you should be willing to update.

Run it forward. Your decisions will become wiser when you consider these three words: "…and then what?"
Most doors swing both ways. Most deciding points are two-way doors, not one-way. Don't get bogged down; you can usually back up.
Halt when impaired. Avoid important decisions when you are Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired (HALT). Just halt when you are HALT.
  • Changing your mind about important things is not a consequence of stupidity, but a sign of intelligence.
  • Strong opinions, clearly stated, but loosely held. Always ask: what would change my mind?
  • When you are right, you are learning nothing.
  • Do not cling to a mistake just because you spent a lot of time making it.

On generosity & relationships

The throughline: generosity is framed not as sacrifice but as the most self-interested thing you can do.

The most selfish thing in the world you can do is to be generous. Your generosity will return you ten fold.
  • If you are more fortunate than others, build a longer table rather than a taller fence.
  • What others want from you is mostly to be seen. Let others know you see them.
  • The very best way to win a friend is to be one.
  • Whenever you hug someone, be the last to let go.
  • You owe everyone a second chance, but not a third.
  • Being envious is a toxin. Take joy in the success of others and treat their success as your gain.

On self, humility & honesty

Humility, in Kelly's framing, is mostly accuracy — being honest about luck and easy to correct.

Honesty as a gift

Always be radically honest, but use your honesty as a gift not a weapon.

Humility as accuracy

Humility is mostly about being very honest about how much you owe to luck.

  • There is a profound difference between thinking less of yourself (not useful), and thinking of yourself less (better).
  • Get good at being corrected without being offended.
  • Admitting that "I don't know" at least once a day will make you a better person.
  • Work on your tone. Often ideas are rejected because of the tone they're wrapped in. Humility covers many blemishes.

On time & the long game

Several of the 101 collapse into one stance: think in long horizons, but act on the nearest small thing.

  • Think in terms of decades, and act in terms of days.
  • Small steps matter more when you play a long game—you compound small advances into large achievements.
  • What is important is seldom urgent and what is urgent is seldom important.
  • The most common mistake we make is to do a great job on an unimportant task.
  • Very small things accumulate until they define your larger life. Carefully choose your everyday things.
  • When you are stuck or overwhelmed, focus on the smallest possible thing that moves your project forward.

On living well

The closing theme is the most personal — what to do with abundance, attention, and the people around you.

The highest form of wealth is deciding you have enough.
  • Don't save up the good stuff (fancy wine, or china) for that rare occasion that will never happen; instead use them whenever you can.
  • There should be at least one thing in your life you enjoy despite being no good at it. This is your play time. Never apologize for it.
  • Discover people whom you love doing "nothing" with, and do nothing with them on a regular basis.
  • Where you live—what city, what country—has more impact on your well being than any other factor.
  • The cheapest therapy is to spend time with people who make you laugh.
  • Write your own obituary, the one you'd like to have, and then everyday work towards making it true.
