How to Become So Creative It Feels Illegal

Brain-fried and out of ideas? Creativity isn't a talent you lack — it's a natural state buried under interference. A diagnosis of what narrows the mind, why "boredom" is really overstimulation, and a 7-day protocol to feel alive again.

The premise

In a world where "anyone can build, think, or write anything," creativity is the most scarce resource — the winners take "the path that nobody else considered to take." Creativity is not "creating something from nothing"; it's a relaxed, open state of consciousness — noticing the unnoticed, seeing connections and possibilities. Everyone has the capacity; it just gets buried.

I — You don't have ideas because there's too much interference

Saying "I'm not a creative person" treats creativity as a skill you lack. But it's a way of being that gets narrowed over time. The three culprits:

Creativity is rarely an input problem… but you can only cook with what's in the fridge. The problem is that most people's fridges are overflowing with ice cream and soda pop.— Dan Koe

II — You're not bored, you're overstimulated

Chronic overstimulation isn't boredom — you're so used to euphoria that calm reads as boring; "you must come back the other way." True boredom (after the withdrawal period) does three things:

All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone.— Naval Ravikant (quoted by Koe)

III — The 7-day protocol to slow down (feel alive again)

It's deliberately simple — which makes people think they're above it and skip it. Don't. ("That's one reason you aren't very creative.")

Day 1–2 · Reduce the input fast (intermittent fasting for the mind). ① Strict timeblocks — limit work to ~4 hrs/day this week; an alarm marks the end, no "one last task," and don't think about work when not working. ② Cut your #1 mindless input (the podcast, the bedtime scroll, the morning news) — replace it with nothing; sit in silence and listen for an idea. ③ Walk — no headphones, even leave the phone; "ideas are caught in motion." → frees the brain's default mode network (random insight, self-reflection) — which can't fire while you're consuming.
Day 3–4 · Digest what's already there. ① Read one chapter slowly — stop when a sentence makes you think and sit with why it hit (that one idea beats finishing the book). ② Sit with nothing for 10 min — no app, no guided breathing; let the mind be chaotic, that's the digestion. ③ Keep walking, but actually see things — the sidewalk's detail, the tree's branches, the sky. → releases your grip on the subconscious so "aha" moments surface. "If those insights lead to higher-leverage work, are you not getting more done by not working?"
Day 5–6 · Become interested in life again. ① Trust ideas will return — resist noting everything ("if it's important, it will find its way to you"); let a thought play out, stay in the stream, don't reach for your phone. ② One real conversation — no 5-minute "catching up," no networking; just be present. ③ Extend the walk. → dopamine receptors resensitize; you do things because they're inherently interesting (a big predictor of creative output).
Day 7 · Create with what's emerged. Create from abundance, not obligation. Make something with no plan — write, draw, a 20-min voice note, cook without a recipe; "the only rule is no rules." Don't share it — feel what it's like to make something yours without silent opinions steering you (post it after 24 hrs if you want). → separates generative thinking (producing ideas) from evaluative thinking (judging them) — doing both at once suppresses the generative.

IV — To be creative, you need something to create

Learn a new word and you hear it everywhere; that's the reticular activating system (RAS) — it filters millions of inputs and surfaces the ones matching what you've internalized as "this matters." So a calm mind isn't enough:

Without a project, your mind is a boat drifting in open water.— Dan Koe

The protocol calms the storm, but you still need direction. With a meaningful project, a sentence in a book or a conversation overheard on the street becomes creative fuel that others wouldn't catch.

The deeper move: "if you are the culmination of the ideas you've accepted, and the ideas you accept are based on what you deem important," then the way to a rare life is simply to question and choose what is important to you — rather than the school/job/retirement script handed down by industrial culture.
