You Don't Need More Discipline; You Need a Better Writing Routine

By Elin (In It For The Plot) · Published Jan 6, 2026 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Writing Routine Consistency Friction Practical

It was never a willpower issue. She was forcing a routine that didn't fit her life — early mornings, a Pinterest-worthy desk, a printed draft — and no amount of willpower fixes that.

The core idea

A routine isn't there to force you to write. It's there to make writing a natural part of your life by reducing friction. So the question to ask isn't "How do I become more disciplined?" — it's "How do I reduce friction between me and what I want to do?" Momentum is built through consistency, not intensity.

01Stop equating routine with discipline

For a long time she thought she struggled with discipline. She'd fall into a story idea, write obsessively for four days, then lose momentum just as quickly. The excitement faded, life caught up, she stopped — and she told herself it was a willpower issue.

It wasn't. She was forcing a routine that didn't fit her life: the early mornings, the Pinterest-worthy desk, the coffee, the printed finished draft — writing as a lifestyle aesthetic. Every failure to stick felt like proof she wasn't serious or good enough. But most of her actual writing happens in bed, late at night, on her phone, between work, life, and being tired. A routine that ignores all of that was never going to hold, no matter how hard she tried.

02Start from your actual life

Before deciding when or how often you'll write, be honest about the life you actually have. Ask:

  • When do I actually have mental space?
  • When am I least distracted — not most motivated?
  • Where do I already open notes, documents, or drafts?

For her, the answer was: in bed, late at night, on her phone — something she'd long dismissed as "not real writing." Instead of "How much should I write?", the better questions are "When does it come naturally to me?" and "Where do I enjoy writing?"

Then let the starts be tiny enough to count:

Opening the document. That's it. That's a session.
Writing one paragraph. Not a chapter — a paragraph.
Sitting with the story for 10 minutes. No output required.
Adding notes instead of prose. Thinking on the page still counts.

Your routine should be easy enough to keep even on low-energy days. That's the whole point.

Momentum is built through consistency — not intensity.

03You need to be bored

She realised she was almost always consuming something: a podcast while cooking, scrolling on the commute, filling the thirty seconds while waiting for anything. There was barely a moment when she wasn't either consuming or working.

So she took a walk — no headphones, no doing anything — and it was surreal how quickly her mind started to wander and brainstorm. Creatives need to be bored. They need mental rest, and space for thoughts to wander. Try a phone-free commute, or a walk without listening to anything. You'll be surprised by what surfaces.

04Choose time over output

Word counts can be motivating — or paralysing. If you tend to freeze, switch your unit from output to time: 15 minutes, 25 minutes, one short session three times a week. Your only commitment is to be there. Some days the words flow, some days they don't — and both still count.

05Not every session looks the same

A session doesn't have to produce pages to be worthwhile. Some are for moving the story forward; others are for figuring out where it's going.

Sessions for output

  • Drafting
  • Moving forward
  • Writing badly on purpose

Sessions for thinking

  • Thinking
  • Outlining
  • Rereading
  • Making notes
Progress doesn't always look productive.

06What a routine is really for

A routine won't make writing "easy." But it will do three quieter, more durable things:

Somewhere to grow
Gives your ideas a place to develop instead of evaporating
Keeps work concrete
Stops your project from turning abstract
Builds trust
With yourself, that you'll keep showing up

And that trust is what finishes books.

If you're struggling to build a routine, you're not failing — you're learning how to meet yourself where you are. That, too, is part of becoming a writer.
