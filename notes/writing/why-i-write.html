Why I Write

By George Orwell•Published 1946 (Gangrel)•Saved Jun 9, 2026
Writing Craft Politics & Language Motivation

Every writer is driven by a tangle of motives — vanity, love of beauty, the urge to record, the urge to change minds — and which one wins says everything about the books they end up writing.

The core

Orwell traces his own writing back to a lonely childhood and an early certainty that he was meant to write. He then names four enduring motives behind all prose — egoism, aesthetic pleasure, the historical impulse, and political purpose — present in everyone but mixed in different proportions, shifting with the age you live in. His own life pushed the political motive to the front, and his life's work became an attempt to fuse it with art: to make political writing beautiful rather than merely true.

The writer was set early

Orwell says he knew from around age five or six that he would be a writer. Between seventeen and twenty-four he tried to give the idea up, but doing so meant betraying his own nature — and the impulse came back. He describes himself as a lonely middle child who invented stories, kept up conversations with imaginary companions, and felt isolated and undervalued. That mix produced two lasting habits: a facility with words, and a willingness to look squarely at unpleasant facts rather than away from them.

A useful distinction follows. A writer's subject matter is handed to them by the age they happen to live in. But their emotional attitude — the temperament they bring to that subject — is set before they ever start writing seriously. Try to escape those early influences entirely, Orwell warns, and you don't free yourself; you kill the very impulse to write.

The four great motives

This is the heart of the essay. Orwell argues that, setting aside the need to earn a living, four motives drive everyone who writes prose. They exist in every writer at once, in proportions that vary from person to person and shift over time as circumstances change. He is candid that they often pull against each other.

How life tipped the balance

By temperament, Orwell says, his first three motives outweighed the fourth. In a peaceful era he might have written ornate, descriptive books and barely noticed his own political loyalties. But the times didn't allow it. His years in the Imperial Police in Burma, his deliberate descent into poverty afterwards, an early sense of failure, the rise of Hitler, and above all the Spanish Civil War of 1936–37 forced the political motive to the front. After 1936, he says, everything serious he wrote was written — directly or indirectly — against totalitarianism and for the kind of democratic socialism he believed in.

Making political writing an art

Orwell's stated aim across his last decade was to turn political writing into an art. The starting point is always partisanship — a sense of injustice, a lie to expose, a fact that deserves attention. But that alone is not enough; the writing has to be an aesthetic experience as well. Animal Farm, he notes, was the first book in which he set out consciously to weld political purpose and artistic purpose into one whole.

He's honest about the cost of that conviction. In Homage to Catalonia, a friendly critic told him a fact-heavy chapter had turned what might have been a good book into journalism. Orwell agreed it hurt the book — but he could not cut it, because innocent men were being falsely accused, and the facts mattered more than the shape of the chapter. The aesthetic instinct and the duty to the truth don't always agree, and he chose the truth.

The discipline of the windowpane

Orwell's ideal of style is transparency — prose that lets you see straight through to the thing being described, with no decoration calling attention to itself.

"Good prose is like a windowpane." — Orwell

To write like that, he argues, you have to keep struggling to efface your own personality. He found the reverse too: wherever he lacked a real political purpose, he slipped into lifeless books and gaudy, "purple" passages.

The honest caveat

Orwell refuses to let the high-minded, public-spirited framing stand as the whole story. All writers, he says flatly, are vain, selfish, and lazy, and at the very bottom of their motives lies a mystery. Writing a book is a horrible, exhausting struggle, driven by a kind of demon one can neither resist nor fully understand. The four motives are real; so is the fact that, beneath them, the writer is also just a person who cannot help themselves.

What to keep

Know which of the four motives is really driving you — and be suspicious of any answer that's purely flattering. Let the age give you your subject, but don't pretend you have no attitude toward it. If you want clear prose, find something true enough to say that it pushes your ego out of the way. And accept that even the noblest stated reasons sit on top of an impulse you'll never entirely explain.
