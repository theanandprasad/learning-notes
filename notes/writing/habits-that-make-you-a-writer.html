Habits That Will Make You a Better Writer

By Elin (In It For The Plot) · Published Apr 8, 2026 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Writing Habits Atomic Habits Consistency Practical

You don't need more discipline or motivation — you need small habits that make writing easier. Stop asking "Why can't I just be more motivated?" and start asking "What would make this easier?"

The core idea

Habits aren't 5am alarms or 2,000-word quotas. They're small structures in your day that reduce friction — they make writing obvious, easy, and tied to things you already do. And the ones that stick are built around the person you actually are, not your idealised self.

01Not discipline — habits

There's a lie that quietly runs in the background: if you really wanted to write, you'd already be doing it. If you were more disciplined, more motivated, more serious, you'd wake earlier, write every day, finish the draft, stop procrastinating. That's a false, manufactured view of productivity — the "watch this before you write" videos and aesthetic Pinterest mood boards that mostly just make you feel bad. Most actual writing doesn't look like that.

By habits, Elin doesn't mean waking at 5am or hitting 2,000 words a day. She means small structures that make writing easier. As Atomic Habits (James Clear) puts it: habits are easier to keep when they are obvious, easy, and tied to things you already do. The easier something is to see and start, the more likely you are to do it.

Open your document
while you drink your morning coffee
Keep your notebook on your pillow
so you see it before bed
A phone note: "story ideas"
somewhere you'll actually find it
Write for ten minutes after dinner
a tiny, repeatable window
Leave your document open
on your laptop, ready to start

02Habit stacking

Another idea from Atomic Habits: habit stacking — attach a new habit to something you already do, so it stops feeling like a big daily decision and starts feeling like brushing your teeth.

The deeper shift is in the question you ask yourself:

Stop asking

"Why can't I just be more motivated?"

A question that points the blame inward and never resolves.

Start asking

"What would make this easier?"

A question you can actually answer — and act on.

03Build habits for who you actually are

The biggest mistake is building habits around the person you wish you were — productive, organised, never tired, never distracted, never overwhelmed — instead of who you actually are. Before deciding on any habit, Elin suggests asking yourself three questions:

  • When do I genuinely have energy?
  • When am I least distracted?
  • What kind of writing feels enjoyable, or easy to return to?

Her honest answers

She needs a coffee and to be outside first thing. She's useless between 5:30 and 7:30pm unless someone tells her exactly what to do. She gets ideas all day and forgets them unless she writes them down. She writes late at night, a lot of it on her phone — and "it's not pretty," but it works.

04Plan for low and high energy days

Not every day is the same, and your habits shouldn't pretend they are. Staying connected to your writing matters more than constantly pushing yourself.

High-energy days

  • Drafting
  • Writing new scenes
  • Brainstorming

Lower-energy days

  • Rereading what you wrote
  • Fixing a sentence
  • Making notes
  • Writing down questions about the plot

You don't need to earn your place as a writer by becoming the image we see online. What works for others doesn't have to work for you. It doesn't have to be aesthetically pleasing — it needs to work for you and make you feel good.

05Write even when it's not exciting

You have a new idea. You're obsessed. Everything feels full of possibility. Then the feeling disappears — the scene isn't working, you're bored of your own idea, you don't know what happens next, and you start wondering if the whole thing is terrible and you should just start something else.

For the longest time, Elin thought this meant something was wrong: that she'd lost interest, that it wasn't the right story, that she wasn't good enough. Now she thinks the opposite.

This is where writing actually begins. The people who finish things are the ones who learn to keep going when it's less exciting.— Elin, In It For The Plot

Habits are what carry you through that stretch. They help you stay on track when motivation is gone — and they help you be kinder to yourself while you do it.
