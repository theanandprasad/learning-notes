How to Write When You Have a Full-Time Job

By Elin (In It For The Plot) · Published Apr 20, 2026 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Writing Habits Productivity Energy Management Practical

Social media makes it look like everyone has endless hours and an aesthetic process. Most of us don't. This is practical advice for the version of you that is tired after work — no gatekeeping, and absolutely no "just wake up at 5am." You fit writing to the life you actually have, not the life the internet says you should have.

The core idea

Meet yourself where you are. Map your real week, learn your natural energy, and scale your effort to high-energy and low-energy days. Staying connected to your writing matters more than constantly pushing yourself — that's the part we don't talk about enough.

This corner of the internet is for when you're tired after work, you have twenty-seven tabs open, dinner still needs making — and you'd really like to write the book. Most people balance writing with a job, or uni, or kids, or chores, or friends, or groceries. So advice from full-time creators often just isn't built for your life. Here's advice that is.

01Start with what you actually want

Before the calendar and the energy tracking, spend some time on direction. What do you actually want from your writing? To finish a book? To write more consistently? To publish on Substack twice a week? To finally become a person who writes instead of someone who keeps saying "I want to write"?

Then write it down as one sentence — and don't skip the second half. The why matters as much as the what, because it's what holds you up when motivation dips (and it always does).

Fill in the blank

I want to ______________ because ______________. (the what) (your why)

For example:

  • "I want to publish on Substack twice a week because I want to build something that is mine."
  • "I want to write more consistently because I miss feeling creative."

02Map your real week

Our minds are wildly overambitious. They plan for a version of us who wakes at 5am, meal preps, journals, runs, and writes 2,000 words before work — all with glowy skin and a perfectly balanced nervous system. That person is not coming. So plan for the one who actually exists.

Take your calendar — Google Calendar, a paper planner, your notes app, whatever — and map everything in a normal week. Not the idealised version. The real one:

  • sleep, meals, work, commuting
  • walks, the gym, coffee with a friend
  • the three episodes you watched because you needed it today
  • and that mysterious forty minutes lost to scrolling — technically doing nothing, but not actually resting either

Then spend a week adjusting it against reality. Maybe you think you have a free hour each evening, but you really have twenty minutes before you become one with the sofa. That's fine. The point isn't to shame yourself — it's to see where your time actually is, and where you might gently re-prioritise.

03Learn your natural energy

Next, spend a few days paying attention to your natural energy levels. Keep a little section in your notes app for the observations. Do you feel more energetic in the morning? Do you focus better in the evening? Are you always a bit useless around lunch? When is your brain sharp, and when is it a cloud?

I'm quite useless between 5 and 7pm unless someone actively tells me what to do — I'll go to a paid workout class because someone expects me there, but I'll never talk myself into a run. Then later in the evening I get a spike of energy and creativity, so evening writing sessions work well for me. — Elin, on tracking her own energy

Where people go wrong is trying to fit writing into a shape they saw online instead of meeting themselves where they are. You do not need to be the TikTok person writing three hours every morning with a candle and a jazz playlist. You need to know your own rhythm and build around it.

04Plan for low and high energy days

Not every day is the same, so stop expecting the same volume from every day. Instead, set different expectations for different kinds of days. The goal is to always have something you can do — so you stay connected even when you're running on empty.

High-energy day

  • Drafting
  • Writing new scenes
  • Brainstorming and generating

Low-energy day

  • Rereading what you wrote
  • Fixing a single sentence
  • Making notes
  • Writing down questions about the plot

Staying connected to your writing matters more than constantly pushing yourself — and that's something we really don't talk about enough. You don't need to do what everyone else does. There are always things we don't see behind the scenes, so don't compare yourself to the polished, aesthetic versions online. Build the practice that fits your actual life, and keep showing up to it.
