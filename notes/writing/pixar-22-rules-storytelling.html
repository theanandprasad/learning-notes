Pixar's 22 Rules of Storytelling

By Dickie Bush (@dickiebush) — on Emma Coats' Pixar rules • Published Jun 15, 2026 • Saved Jun 15, 2026
Storytelling Story Spine Craft Pixar

The single most powerful skill to advance your career is storytelling — and no one tells better stories than Pixar. In 2011, Pixar storyboard artist Emma Coats shared their 22 Rules. Here they are, with Dickie Bush's notes on applying them to writing, brands, and business.

The one to steal first

If you take one thing, take the story spine (Rule #4) — a fill-in-the-blanks scaffold any writer, founder, or brand can use to build a narrative effortlessly. The rest sharpen it: start from the ending, add constraints, give characters opinions, get it on paper, and strip to the essence.

The 22 rules — verbatim

Emma Coats' original wording (2011):

#Rule
1You admire a character for trying more than for their successes.
2You gotta keep in mind what's interesting to you as an audience, not what's fun to do as a writer. They can be very different.
3Trying for theme is important, but you won't see what the story is actually about til you're at the end of it. Now rewrite.
4Once upon a time there was ___. Every day, ___. One day ___. Because of that, ___. Because of that, ___. Until finally ___.
5Simplify. Focus. Combine characters. Hop over detours. You'll feel like you're losing valuable stuff but it sets you free.
6What is your character good at, comfortable with? Throw the polar opposite at them. Challenge them. How do they deal?
7Come up with your ending before you figure out your middle. Seriously. Endings are hard, get yours working up front.
8Finish your story, let go even if it's not perfect. In an ideal world you have both, but move on. Do better next time.
9When you're stuck, make a list of what WOULDN'T happen next. Lots of times the material to get you unstuck will show up.
10Pull apart the stories you like. What you like in them is a part of you; you've got to recognize it before you can use it.
11Putting it on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, a perfect idea, you'll never share it with anyone.
12Discount the 1st thing that comes to mind. And the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th – get the obvious out of the way. Surprise yourself.
13Give your characters opinions. Passive/malleable might seem likable to you as you write, but it's poison to the audience.
14Why must you tell THIS story? What's the belief burning within you that your story feeds off of? That's the heart of it.
15If you were your character, in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty lends credibility to unbelievable situations.
16What are the stakes? Give us a reason to root for the character. What happens if they don't succeed? Stack the odds against.
17No work is ever wasted. If it's not working, let go and move on — it'll come back around to be useful later.
18You have to know yourself: the difference between doing your best & fussing. Story is testing, not refining.
19Coincidences to get characters into trouble are great. But coincidences to get them out of it are cheating.
20Exercise: take the building blocks of a movie you dislike. How would you rearrange them into what you DO like?
21You gotta identify with your situation/characters, can't just write 'cool'. What would make YOU act that way?
22What's the essence of your story? Most economical telling of it? If you know that, you can build from there.

Dickie's standout applications

#1 — Admire the trying. Audiences love the path, the ups and downs. For stories, brands, and products, focus on the how and why (less on the what).
#2 — The writer is irrelevant. Echoes Craig Clemens's first rule of writing: "You, the writer, are completely irrelevant." Focus on delivering to the audience.
#5 — Do more with less. "Add constraints." The goal for every writer, marketer, or entrepreneur is to do more with less.
#7 — Start with the end in mind. Stories, sales pages, brand pitches — always work backward from the ending.
#9 — Beat writer's block. List everything that wouldn't happen next to get the juices flowing.
#11 — Get it out of your head. "Your head is the worst place to store ideas." Tim Ferriss commits to 2 crappy pages per day — focus on getting the raw material.
#12 — Unclog the faucet. Julian Shapiro's Creativity Faucet: to reach your best ideas, drain the obvious junk first.
#13 — Take a stand. "No 'it seems' or 'maybes' or 'potentiallys'." Strong opinions make the audience take a side.
#16 — Stakes are the foundation. Give us a reason to root for them; stack the odds against. (See Matthew Dicks's Storyworthy.)
#22 — Strip to the essence. Ask what you're really trying to say in as few words as possible, then cut everything that distracts from it.

The lines worth keeping

No book is ever finished, it is merely abandoned.— on Rule #8: don't let perfectionism keep you from publishing
90% of being a good writer or storyteller is being a good noticer.— on Rule #10: notice when things resonate, then ask why

And on Rule #18 — "Story is testing, not refining" — Dickie's gloss: planning every step from A to Z is "really procrastination in disguise." Pair it with Rule #17: "No work is ever wasted… focus on showing up — consistency creates competence."
