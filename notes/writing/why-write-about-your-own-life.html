Why You Should Start Writing About Your Own Life

By Elin (In It For The Plot) · Published Apr 7, 2026 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Personal Writing Columns Reflection Self-Expression

Enter your columnist era. In an age where everything passes in a scroll, take a picture so you'll remember where you once were — but write, so you reflect on where you are.

The core idea

Start a personal column. Self-expression and media literacy have never been more precious than they are now. Writing about your own life helps you understand yourself — and your ordinary life is already interesting enough to be worth it.

If you ever watched Sex and the City, or the early-2000s rom-coms where everyone was a journalist with an impossibly exciting life captured in spellbinding quotes — consider this a pep talk to create your own column and step into your columnist era.

There's a saying: "take a picture, so you'll remember where you once were." Take it further — write, so you reflect on where you are. In an age where everything passes in a scroll, it's easy to lose your thoughts and moments in a feed. You have a thought, and before you've finished thinking it, someone on TikTok is already telling you what to think; we scroll past a hundred opinions before forming our own. And yet there has never been more craving for written connection — to ourselves and to each other.

01What is a column?

Essentially, a column is a piece of writing that returns regularly, centred on one topic, thought, or theme. It can be funny, serious, reflective, opinionated. It can be about almost anything you keep coming back to:

You don't need all the answers. The most interesting columns often explore and learn as they go. A few premises to show what that looks like:

  • "I've become obsessed with history documentaries and here is everything I've learnt."
  • "I'm trying to get better at making friends as an adult."
  • "I watched ten Nancy Meyers films and now I have thoughts." (This is me.)

The early-2000s blog days are back — except instead of glittery Blogspot themes and blurry photos, it's now Substack and Notes-app essays.

02Writing helps you understand yourself

Writing reveals things to you. So many thoughts are ones you only realise you were having because you wrote them down. You set out to write one thing and discover it was secretly about another.

That's why everyone should try writing about their life — you never know where the thought might lead.

03Your life is already interesting enough

We imagine writers have more glamorous lives than the rest of us — constantly falling in love in bookshops, moving to Paris, having mysterious encounters in the rain. Which feels a little unfair, because how often does any of that actually happen?

Your life doesn't need to be dramatic to be worth writing about. Some of the best writing comes from the smaller things: the coffee shop you always go to, the friend you miss, the version of yourself you're trying to become. Those are stories too.

A light aside

Or you could do it like Shonda Rhimes: lie. That, she half-joked, is the secret behind her success as a writer — as she put it in her book Year of Yes (a great read).

04One day you'll be glad you wrote it down

One day we'll all be older, and there will be versions of us that no longer exist. There's a quiet comfort in being able to go back to your previous thoughts, interests, and experiences, and see where you were then.

Not because every thought is profound, but because one day you'll be so glad you took the time to pay attention. — Elin, In It For The Plot
