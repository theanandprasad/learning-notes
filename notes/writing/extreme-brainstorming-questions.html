Extreme Brainstorming Questions

By Jason Cohen (A Smart Bear)•Published A Smart Bear (undated)•Saved Jun 9, 2026
Brainstorming Ideas Strategy Creativity

Ordinary brainstorming fails because we're blinded by our daily work: no forest, all trees. The fix is to stretch one dimension of reality so far it's almost nonsense — and watch new ideas fall out.

The mechanic

Take any dimension of your business — price, scope, headcount, the existence of customers — and crank it to an absurd extreme. The point isn't to act on the absurdity. It's that a dramatically different perspective forces ideas you'd never reach from inside your normal constraints. An idea that would only be a 60% solution in the extreme hypothetical can be a 2x or even 10x idea back in reality. And sometimes the extreme is the strategy — it unlocks a long-term moat.

Why does this work? Because most of the genuinely transformative business models looked impossible right up until someone shipped them. Zappos let you return shoes free, 364 days later. Robinhood made stock trades cost $0. Amazon Prime made delivery free. Netflix mailed you DVDs. Open-source companies charge money for software that's free and open. Airbnb, Uber, and Lyft are built entirely on strangers trusting strangers. Each one sounds like a brainstorm someone should have laughed out of the room — until it became the default.

How to use the prompts

Each question below picks one dimension and pushes it to a breaking point. Answer it honestly inside the hypothetical, then ask the real question: which part of that answer would still be valuable if I dialed the extreme back to normal? That residue is where the new strategy lives. The fifteen prompts group into five families.

Pricing & business model

1 · 10x prices

If you were forced to raise prices tenfold, what would you have to do to justify it? Rethink brand, positioning, the narrow subset of customers who'd happily pay, premium services worth that much. You won't actually charge 10x — but you may discover a clean path to at least 2x.

2 · No customers

Imagine every customer vanished and you rebuilt from scratch, free of all legacy expectations. What unique message, positioning, pricing, and feature set would you choose? Build for the 10x-larger pool of future customers, not the ones you happen to have inherited.

7 · Flipped business model

You're forced to charge in a completely different manner — usage-based becomes flat-rate, tiers become a per-day formula. The change forces you to re-examine how you frame value, where your costs actually sit, and which features matter under the new economics.

11 · Cost is no object

Unprofitability doesn't matter; spend 10–100x freely. Treat the product as philanthropy. The trick: a high-touch concierge experience done 30% by software at 1/100th the cost is a genuinely good feature you can ship in reality.

Product & scope

3 · No tech support

You're never allowed to provide support. That forces self-serve onboarding, more user control, better visibility into state, and more intuitive actions. The payoff is real on both sides: self-service is more profitable and makes customers happier.

4 · Maximize fun

What's the most fun thing you could build — that still improves the product? Run a one-week hackathon on it. Fun isn't a distraction from productivity; it's a source of it, plus fulfillment.

6 · No time

Ship a complete, lovable MVP/SLC feature in two weeks. Strip all complexity, minimize scope — and get more learning on day one than a six-month plan delivers in six months.

"To achieve great things, two things are needed: a plan, and not quite enough time." — Leonard Bernstein

Growth & distribution

5 · Complete rip-off

Your biggest competitor copies every single feature. How do you still win? The answer is never a feature — features always get copied. It's ease and design, the speed at which you ship the next feature, brand, mission, trust, service, community.

8 · No website

Grow with no website at all. That pushes you toward word-of-mouth, advocates and ambassadors, social, and product-as-marketing — growth that lives inside the product itself.

10 · No customer contact

You may never talk to a customer. So you lean on behavioral measurement, fast A/B tests, "coming soon" buttons to gauge demand, and analysis of what people say online. Not a pure loss — interviews can mislead anyway.

Org & process

9 · No meetings

No synchronous meetings, ever. Onboarding, decisions, and brainstorming all move to async systems — and the ideas may be better without groupthink in the room. Ask what documents would have to exist: e.g. "the why" behind your last twenty decisions, written down.

12 · Sociopathic CEO

Change anything, regardless of whose feelings it hurts. Explore terminating a product line, or pivoting the whole company. The best ideas shouldn't be dodged just because they're hard — and you can often execute them by reorganizing rather than laying off.

Strategy & survival

13 · Mortal wound

What single externality could kill the entire company — a competitor's product or price, a security breach, an economic shift? Name it, then mitigate it. Cohen ran this at WP Engine and surfaced 35 existential threats. You don't fix all of them, but some of the fixes are wise regardless.

14 · Philanthropist

If your only goal were to create the most good for customers — money aside — what would you do? Help small businesses thrive, help writers improve their craft, help performers get attention. Mission beats metrics for loyalty, and Gen Z notices.

15 · Only one thing this year

If you could ship exactly one thing all year, what single initiative makes the most difference? Often a whole year's trajectory comes down to one or two big decisions.

"The dangerous man is the one who has only one idea… The way real science goes is you come up with lots of ideas, and most of them will be wrong." — Francis Crick

The payoff curve

The reason the absurdity is worth tolerating: the value doesn't disappear when you leave the hypothetical. It scales. What looks like a partial answer under impossible constraints becomes an outsized advantage under normal ones — and once in a while, you find you can just keep the extreme and turn it into a moat.

Closing

Cohen ends by asking readers to subscribe and to share the piece with anyone who could use a fresh angle on a stuck problem — fitting for an essay whose whole point is that the best ideas come from a perspective you'd never choose on your own.
