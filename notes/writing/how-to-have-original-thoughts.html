How to Start Having Original Thoughts

By Feifei (Feifei's Substack) • Published Mar 9, 2026 • Saved Jun 5, 2026
Thinking Creativity Attention Self-Expression

You haven't lost your ability to think. You've just been outsourcing it so long the muscle has weakened. The fix isn't becoming a different person — it's practicing a different behavior.

The core idea

Original thinking is a skill, not a trait, and it's synthesis, not invention from nothing. The reason you don't have original thoughts is that your environment produces zero friction between you and what you consume. Deliberately introducing friction is the practice.

It's a skill, not a trait

Original thinking isn't a personality type reserved for artists and contrarians with interesting glasses. It's a capacity — and like most capacities, it atrophies when unused and develops when used. The bad news: practicing it feels terrible at first. (The author is candid that she writes about this because she spent a long time not having original thoughts — "most people who write about original thinking have spent significant time not having any.")

It's synthesis, not invention from nothing

People imagine original thinking is sitting alone and producing a fully-formed brilliant idea no one's had. It isn't. It's taking things you've encountered and running them through your own life until they come out changed. Nobody thinks in a vacuum.

The difference between original thinkers and regurgitators isn't that one never consumes others' ideas — it's that one group does something with them: pushes back, extends the argument somewhere the author didn't go, notices where it breaks down against their own experience, sits with it long enough that it gets fingerprints on it. Your fingerprints. That's what makes it yours.

The practice: introduce friction

Everything is smooth: you scroll, absorb, agree, share, move on — never required to do anything with what you took in. Original thinking requires friction.

The central drill

After you read / watch / listen to something — before you look at what anyone else thinks — write down what you actually thought. Not the smart take. Not what you're supposed to think. What you actually noticed, what bothered you, what you believed before and whether it changed. Catch your raw reaction before the internet gets to it.

It doesn't have to be good or coherent. In fact, if it sounds polished and certain, you're probably still performing rather than thinking.

Sit with not-knowing

You'll often find you don't know what you think. That's not failure — that's exactly the right place to start. The discomfort of not knowing is where actual thought begins. Most people flee it instantly by reaching for their phone to find out what they're supposed to think. Don't. Sit there a little longer.

Half-formed thoughts are not shameful thoughts. They're thoughts in progress.

Start saying — out loud or in writing — "I'm not sure why, but I don't think I agree with that." Then try to figure out why. The trying is the thinking. The answer at the end is almost secondary.

Right vs. persuasive

When you watch a video essay and catch yourself nodding — stop. Ask whether you're nodding because the person is right or because they're persuasive. The only way to tell the difference is to know what you thought before they started talking.

Why this is worth the discomfort

Original thinking means putting forward something traceable back to you — so if it's wrong, unpopular, or half-baked, you own it. You can't shrug and say "that's what everyone was saying." That vulnerability is exactly what you've been avoiding — and exactly what makes real connection possible.

People feel close to you when you say something unmistakably, recognizably you — a strange connection no one else would make, a real push-back, an honest "I don't know."

The flat conversations — the ones where people weren't that interested — were probably flat because there was no resistance in you.

Read slower, read less

One book genuinely wrestled with beats twenty consumed to say you'd read them. Annotation makes a text argue with you: write in the margins, "why do I agree or disagree?", "this reminds me of X and I'm not sure they're compatible." When you finish — before Goodreads, before reviews — write a paragraph: What did this change in how I see something? What did I think it would argue vs. what it actually argued? What would I push back on?

The long game

Developing your own perspective is a years-long project — rebuilding a habit of mind that slowly eroded. There'll be stretches where you sit down to write what you think and come up empty, and it'll feel like proof there's nothing there. But there's something there. It's just rusty. You're not trying to become someone else — you're trying to find out who you already are underneath the borrowed opinions. Be quiet long enough to hear them. Keep going anyway.

PS — "for the love of God, stop opening the comments section of every video before you come to an opinion yourself."
