How to Articulate Yourself Intelligently

By Dan Koe · Published Nov 20, 2025 · Saved Jun 5, 2026
Communication Writing Frameworks Thinking

Being articulate isn't a sign of raw intelligence you're born with — it's a capacity you build on top of a refined body of work. The people who sound brilliant on demand have simply thought through their best ideas hundreds of times.

Core idea

Build an Inner Album of Greatest Hits — 8–10 big ideas you've thought through so many times you can connect them to almost any topic — then use 3 frameworks to articulate them on demand. Repeating your best ideas isn't a flaw; it's a feature, the same way a musician has a recognisable signature sound. The most important ideas are the ones that deserve repeating, and repetition is how you refine them.

01Articulation is a skill, not a trait

When Dan Koe was young, he admired articulate thinkers like Alan Watts, Jordan Peterson, and Daniel Schmachtenberger, and assumed they were simply more intelligent than everyone else. Over a decade later — without really trying to — he became someone people ask: how do you write and speak so well?

The realisation: being good at tests is not the same as stringing together coherent thoughts and articulating them clearly. Articulation is a capacity built over a body of work, not an innate gift. And it's not that difficult to do once you understand where it comes from.

02The Inner Album of Greatest Hits

To articulate intelligently you need a pool of 8–10 of your biggest ideas — ideas you can connect to almost any topic because you've already thought them through hundreds of times. They give you a starting point so you're never building from scratch in the moment.

Koe has written 2 books, thousands of posts, and hundreds of newsletters and videos. It became obvious which ideas were most valuable — the ones with the most views and the most "this changed my life" DMs. In truth he really only has 8–10 big ideas, and together they represent his brand. Jordan Peterson's articulation works the same way: it flows from his body of work.

The hurdle is the fear of sounding repetitive. So on podcasts Koe would avoid his best material, go blank, and try to invent a mind-blowing idea on the spot — which is not how ideas work. Ideas require time to dissect and refine; you can't manufacture a great one live.

The musician analogy

You listen to a favourite musician because of a specific sound or style. Most of their songs sound the same with slight variations — and you can recognise the artist within a few seconds. If an EDM artist suddenly switched to country, the first track would be horrible and they'd lose their audience. In the same way, you must write and speak thousands of times until your best ideas become obvious. By nature you must repeat yourself — because the most important ideas deserve repeating, and that's exactly how you refine them.

Big ideas as tweets

Think of your big ideas as tweets organised under content pillars. For each pillar, Koe keeps a few short tweets that hit hard:

Lead with your best idea

The best podcast guests don't answer the question directly. They speak their best idea on that topic with confidence, then expand with supporting points. It keeps listeners engaged — and it becomes a clippable, compounding moment.

Asked "What's the greatest skill you can learn?", Alex Hormozi wouldn't just say "sales." He'd respond with:

The single greatest skill you can develop is the ability to stay in a great mood in the absence of things to be in a great mood about. — Alex Hormozi

It's novel and it sets up an interesting conversation. And because that tweet already had roughly 105k likes, reusing it isn't lazy — it's compounding. A proven idea, clipped again, keeps paying off.

03Start by writing

If you don't know what to learn, start writing. Writing teaches you how to think, how to learn, and how to inspire people to care. Koe calls himself a writer but doesn't really consider himself one — he doesn't care about grammar, cleverness, or run-on sentences — yet millions follow him.

You already write every day: texts, emails, proposals, feedback. And the foundation of media — how work gets in front of people and persuades them — is writing. Attention lives on social, YouTube, podcasts, and ads, all of which require articulate writing: scripts, posts, sales copy, captions.

He recommends a 1–2 hour morning writing habit (the basis of his "2 Hour Writer"). Posting publicly then gives you feedback — engagement shows which ideas land hardest — and building an audience gives you free distribution.

043 Frameworks (beginner → advanced)

① Beginner — The Micro Story

The mind is a story engine; humans can't help paying attention to a short, impactful story. The foundation of any story is transformation — which can be as simple as problem → solution. The problem plus its amplification is the hook. It's classic copywriting, and still Koe's go-to.

② Intermediate — The Pyramid Principle

Structure ideas hierarchically and logically so they're palatable and persuasive. It's answer-first — the opposite of content that withholds the answer until the end. Start with your main idea, support it with 3–5 key arguments, then back each with detailed evidence.

③ Advanced — Cross-Domain Synthesis

Koe's favourite, drawing on his multiple interests — psychology, philosophy, business, design, tech, health. You set up a problem, then pull a concept from one of your other domains to illuminate it, teaching the audience something new, before laying out your own process.

05The Lego blocks of writing

When you're stuck filling out a section, cycle through these idea-forms — the legos that compose most of Koe's outlines — until it becomes second nature.

Pain point — start a section with a relevant pain the reader feels.
Example — grounds what you're saying; can go almost anywhere.
Personal story — a time in your life that relates to the point.
Statistic — a truthful stat adds authority.
Metaphor — explain a complex idea simply, the way Alan Watts did.
Quote — almost always a great idea, ready-made.
Reframe — offer a different perspective on the point.
What / How / Why — when stuck, ask. Thinking is questioning.
