Prompts That Rewrite Themselves

By Hanako (@hanakoxbt) • Published Jun 19, 2026 • Saved Jun 26, 2026
Prompt Engineering Self-Improving Systems Claude Evaluator-Apprentice

One of Anthropic's clients got 2,740 applications for a single role in 24 hours. No hand-written prompt stays right that long — so stop writing it once and start building a system that rewrites it for you.

The key idea

A recruiter starts with a prompt: "five years of backend, startup background, strong Python." Two days in, after the first batch, they realize they actually want zero-to-one builders. The prompt is already wrong before they've read the second hundred. The teams shipping fastest don't hand-tune words — they build systems where the prompt watches what the user actually decides and rewrites itself in the background. By the time the user notices their preferences have shifted, the prompt has already shifted with them. This works anywhere a human makes judgment calls and the model helps: recruiting, support triage, content moderation, code review, deal scoring.

Why a fixed prompt rots

The problem isn't that your first prompt is bad. It's that the world it describes moves faster than you can re-type. When 2,740 applications land in a day, the recruiter's taste is being refined every few minutes — but the prompt that filters those applications is frozen at whatever you wrote on Monday morning. The gap between "what the prompt says" and "what the user is actually choosing" only widens.

Most teams treat the prompt as a deliverable they finish. The teams that ship the most treat it as a layer that's always learning. The rest of this note is the four-piece machine that makes that possible.

Piece 1 — Batch the learning, don't learn on every click

The instinct is to update the prompt the moment a user makes a decision. Resist it. As one speaker, "Nick," put it on stage: you start spotting patterns every 100 to 200 decisions, not on every click. One rejected CV is noise; a hundred of them is a signal.

There's a cost reason too. Running a smart model on every single action burns your budget by lunchtime. Running it on batches keeps the system alive in production. Batching is both more accurate and cheaper — the rare alignment where the right call is also the frugal one.

Rule of thumb

Patterns emerge around 100–200 decisions. Below that, you're chasing noise. Pick a batch size and learn on the batch, never on the click.

Piece 2 — Split into two layers, not one giant agent

The tempting design is one giant agent that evaluates inputs, learns from decisions, and rewrites the prompt all at once. It doesn't scale and it burns tokens. The working pattern is two layers with very different jobs.

The evaluator — bottom layer

Cheap, fast, runs on EVERY input. In the recruiting case that's Haiku scoring every CV against the current profile — thousands a day. Its job is narrow: take the input plus the current prompt, return a structured judgment. Nothing else.

The apprentice — top layer

Slower, smarter, runs RARELY. It watches only the decisions humans make. Every batch it asks one question: does the prompt still match what the user is actually choosing? If no, it rewrites it.

Most teams skip the split. They put a frontier model in the hot path on every request, the bill explodes, and the system gets shelved before it proves itself.

Splitting evaluation from learning is what keeps it alive in production.

Piece 3 — Write the prompt in prose, not in rules

The engineer's instinct is a config: 30% weight on years of experience, 20 on company tier, 10 on education, a handful of keyword flags. It feels rigorous. It's a dead end. The model can't update it — there's nothing to update but numbers — and numbers don't capture why a recruiter said no.

The working format is plain-English markdown. Sentences the apprentice can actually edit:

The prompt — written as proseWe want someone who shipped a product from zero to one, ideally at a startup under fifty people. Strong engineering culture matters more than a specific tech stack. Red flag: only worked at companies bigger than a thousand people.

That's a prompt the apprentice can work with — add a sentence, remove one, tighten a phrase. You can't do any of that to a weighted scoring rubric. Prose is the only format that's both human-readable and machine-rewritable.

Config / rubric

30% experience, 20% company tier, 10% education, keyword flags. Looks rigorous. Nothing to rewrite but numbers, and the numbers never explain the why.

Plain-English prose

"Shipped zero to one at a startup under fifty." Captures intent and reasoning. The apprentice can add, cut, and tighten lines as taste moves.

Piece 4 — The feedback loop is the whole product

Once those three pieces are in place, the system runs on its own. The user never thinks about the prompt — they just keep making the calls only a human can make, and the prompt updates underneath them.

User makes decisions. Real human judgment calls accumulate — the only signal that matters.
Evaluator scores against the current prompt. Cheap and constant, on every input.
Every 100 decisions, the apprentice reads the pile and rewrites the prompt. The expensive step, run rarely.
The next batch is evaluated against the new version. The loop closes; the prompt stays current by construction.

Most teams treat the prompt as a deliverable they finish. The teams that ship the most treat it as a layer that's always learning. Build it that way from day one and you stop spending weeks tuning words — you spend them shipping the product.
