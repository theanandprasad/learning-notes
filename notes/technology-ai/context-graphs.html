AI's Trillion-Dollar Opportunity: Context Graphs

By Jaya Gupta & Ashu Garg (Foundation Capital) • Published Dec 22, 2025 • Saved Jun 15, 2026
AI Agents Systems of Record Context Graph Enterprise

The last generation of enterprise software became trillion-dollar by owning the canonical data — Salesforce, Workday, SAP. The next won't come from adding AI to that data. It'll come from capturing the layer no system ever stored: the decision traces that explain why things happened.

The thesis

Agents become the interface, but something still has to be canonical underneath. The half everyone discusses is better access + governance to data that already exists. The missing half is the context graph: a living record of decision traces — exceptions, overrides, precedents, cross-system context — that today lives in Slack threads, deal desks, escalation calls, and people's heads. Whoever captures it owns the next trillion-dollar platform: a system of record for decisions, not just objects.

Rules vs decision traces

The piece builds on Jamin Ball's "Long Live Systems of Record" — agents don't replace systems of record, they raise the bar. The authors agree, then go further: Ball assumes the data agents need already lives somewhere and just needs better access, semantic contracts, and rules. That's half the picture. The other half is the reasoning that connects data to action — which was never stored as data at all.

A context graph: not "the model's chain-of-thought," but a living record of decision traces stitched across entities and time so precedent becomes searchable… it explains not just what happened, but why it was allowed to happen.

What systems of record don't capture

Agents are shipping into real workflows — contract review, quote-to-cash, support resolution — and hitting a wall governance alone can't solve. The wall isn't missing data; it's missing decision traces. Four things that live nowhere durable:

Exception logic in people's heads. "We always give healthcare companies an extra 10% because their procurement cycles are brutal." Not in the CRM — tribal knowledge passed down in onboarding and side conversations.
Precedent from past decisions. "We structured a similar deal for Company X last quarter — we should be consistent." No system links the two deals or records why the structure was chosen.
Cross-system synthesis. The support lead checks ARR in Salesforce, sees two open escalations in Zendesk, reads a Slack churn-risk thread, and escalates. That synthesis happens in their head. The ticket just says "escalated to Tier 3."
Approval chains outside systems. A VP approves a discount on a Zoom call or a Slack DM. The opportunity record shows the final price — not who approved the deviation, or why.
This is what "never captured" means. Not that the data is dirty or siloed, but that the reasoning connecting data to action was never treated as data in the first place.

The context graph in practice

When startups instrument the agent orchestration layer to emit a decision trace on every run, they get a structured, replayable history of how context turned into action. The article's worked example:

The feedback loop is what compounds. Captured traces become searchable precedent; every automated decision adds another trace. None of this requires full autonomy on day one — it starts human-in-the-loop (the agent proposes, gathers context, routes approvals, records the trace). Even when a human still makes the call, the graph keeps growing instead of letting the rationale die in Slack.

Why incumbents can't build it

Ball is optimistic incumbents evolve — warehouses become "truth registries," CRMs become "state machines with APIs." That works for making existing data accessible. It doesn't work for capturing decision traces.

That's the context graph, and that will be the single most valuable asset for companies in the era of AI.

The question isn't whether systems of record survive — they will. It's whether the next trillion-dollar platforms are built by adding AI to existing data, or by capturing the decision traces that make data actionable.
