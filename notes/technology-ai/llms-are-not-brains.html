LLMs Are Not Brains, and What's Actually Missing for AGI

By Jyothi Venkat (@jyothiwrites) · Published Apr 9, 2026 · Saved Jun 6, 2026
AGI LLMs Neuroscience AI Research

Everyone says we're close to AGI. LLMs are incredible tools — but the gap between predicting the next word and actually understanding the world is wider than most realize. To see why, you have to look at how brains work, how LLMs work, and the five concrete things that stand between them.

Core idea

Brains aren't a tidy "layer cake." They're interconnected subsystems running in parallel. LLMs share some computational principles with brains, but differ fundamentally in how they're built and how they learn. Five concrete gaps stand between today's LLMs and AGI: no body, no memory, no causal reasoning, no world model, and backwards learning.

Brains aren't a layer cake

The popular story goes: the brain evolved in layers. A "reptile brain" at the bottom for survival, a "mammal brain" in the middle for emotion, and a shiny "human brain" on top for thinking. It's neat, intuitive, and wrong.

Cesario, Johnson & Eisthen (2020), in a paper memorably titled "Your Brain Is Not an Onion With a Tiny Reptile Inside," dismantled the myth. Mammals didn't evolve by stacking new parts on top of old ones. Mammals and reptiles branched from a common ancestor, and both lineages changed substantially since. There is no preserved lizard core running the basement.

What you actually have is a set of interconnected subsystems, all talking to each other constantly:

This is why you sometimes wake up understanding something that confused you yesterday: the hippocampus has been quietly replaying and consolidating it overnight. The brain isn't a pipeline that hands work cleanly from one stage to the next. It's a city with constant traffic in all directions.

How LLMs actually work

At their core, large language models do one thing: predict the next word. You feed in a sequence of text; the model computes a probability over every possible next token, picks one (with a dash of randomness), and repeats. Every ChatGPT or Claude response is this loop run thousands of times.

1. Read the sequence. Take everything so far as context.
2. Score every candidate token. Compute a probability distribution over the whole vocabulary.
3. Pick one. Usually the likeliest, with some sampling randomness.
4. Append and repeat. The chosen token becomes part of the input for the next step.

What makes this work so well is attention: instead of reading strictly left to right one word at a time, every word can look at every other word in the context and weigh how relevant it is.

And scale matters — in a precise way. DeepMind's 2022 Chinchilla work (Hoffmann et al.) showed that a 70B-parameter model trained on the right amount of data (~1.4 trillion words) beats models 4× its size trained on too little data. More parameters and more data, balanced correctly, give better predictions — and these improvements fall on clean, predictable mathematical curves.

A surprising overlap

Research comparing 14 LLMs against human fMRI scans found that the middle layers of LLMs match human brain activity better than the final output layers. The intuition: final layers over-optimize for producing the answer, and in doing so they lose the brain-like, middle-of-the-network processing. So LLMs and brains share some computational principles — which is not the same as working the same way.

The five gaps

Shared principles aside, here is what actually stands between a next-word predictor and a mind:

1 · No Body

You learned what heavy means by picking things up, and what hot means by touching a stove. An LLM learned those words from sentences about them. A 2025 Nature Human Behaviour study found that LLMs align well with humans in abstract domains (law, math) but diverge significantly in sensorimotor domains that require a body — the classic grounding problem. Without a body, an LLM's understanding of the physical world is entirely secondhand.

2 · No Memory

The hippocampus records experiences and consolidates them, especially during sleep, so a single event can teach you a lesson you carry for decades. LLMs have no equivalent. Every conversation starts from scratch; the model cannot learn from the interaction you just had with it. The only way to update it is to retrain the entire model on new data — millions of dollars and weeks of compute. There is no mechanism for a single experience to change the model.

3 · No Causal Reasoning

LLMs are excellent pattern matchers. GPT-4 scores ~97% on tasks asking it to identify causal relationships described in text. But that's pattern-matching on how causal language is used — not actual causal understanding. On genuinely novel scenarios that require intervention and counterfactual thinking ("what would happen if I changed this one thing?"), performance drops sharply. Researchers call it shallow causal reasoning: recognizing causal patterns from training data without reasoning about cause and effect from first principles.

4 · No World Model

Yann LeCun has argued for years that next-token prediction is fundamentally insufficient. His alternative is JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture): instead of predicting the next word or pixel, learn to predict abstract representations of what comes next.

The thrown-ball analogy

If someone throws a ball, you don't predict every pixel of its trajectory — you have an internal physics model that tells you roughly where it lands. An LLM can describe a ball being thrown in perfect English, but has no internal representation of what throwing actually means.

5 · Learning Is Backwards

LLMs learn via backpropagation: compute the error at the output, then send correction signals backward through every layer. This requires a global view of the whole system. Brains don't do this — synapses adjust on local activity, not global error. A synapse strengthens when the neurons on both sides fire together.

LLM — Backpropagation

Error measured at the output, corrections pushed backward through every layer. Needs a global view of the entire network.

Brain — Local rules

Synapses adjust on local signals — neurons that fire together wire together. No global error signal required.

The twist: a 2025 Nature paper found brains do use gradient-like optimization — but through local molecular mechanisms. NMDA receptors register local signals, and CaMKII acts as a kind of molecular memory. The brain achieves something like backprop, through a completely different and more efficient mechanism.

What would it take?

Three paths are being explored to close the gaps — none of them "just make it bigger."

Neurosymbolic AI combines the pattern recognition of neural nets with the logic and rules of symbolic systems. Embodied AI grounds models in the physical world — Meta showed that an embodied LLM (ELLMER) can use force and visual feedback to finish tasks a text-only model cannot. Program synthesis aims at learning abstractions rather than memorizing: Chollet's ARC benchmark tests whether a system can solve a puzzle it has never seen, where today's LLMs lag despite excelling at recall.

The honest close

LLMs are the best pattern matchers ever built, and they are genuinely useful. But AGI needs a body, a memory, a world model, causal reasoning, and a fundamentally different way to learn. On all five, today's systems fall short.

We are not close. And that is okay. Knowing the gaps is how you close them.
